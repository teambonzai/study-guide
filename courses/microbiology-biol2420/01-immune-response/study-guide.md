## 1. What immunology is

**Immunology** = the study of the body's **second and third lines of defense**. *(Slide 3)*

- **1st line** (not the focus here): skin, mucous membranes — physical/chemical barriers.
- **2nd line** (innate): phagocytes, inflammation, fever, complement — fast, non-specific.
- **3rd line** (adaptive): B and T lymphocytes, antibodies — slower, specific, has **memory**.

A healthy ("immunocompetent") immune system does three jobs *(Slide 3)*:
1. **Surveillance** — patrols the whole body.
2. **Recognition** — spots foreign material.
3. **Destruction** — tags or destroys anything deemed foreign.

> **Plain-language:** think of the immune system as building security that (1) walks every
> hallway, (2) checks everyone's ID badge, and (3) removes anyone without a valid badge.

## 2. Self vs. Non-self — the core idea 🎯

White blood cells tell **self** from **non-self** by reading **markers on cell surfaces**. *(Slide 4)*

- **Self** = belongs to you. **Every cell in your body carries "self" markers… except red blood cells.**
- **Non-self** = foreign. A microbe, a splinter, a toxin, a transplanted organ — anything from outside.
- **Non-self = antigen.** These two words mean the same thing. *(Transcript)*

If a patrolling cell reads valid **self** markers → it leaves the cell alone.
If the markers are missing/foreign → the cell is **tagged for destruction or engulfed and destroyed**.

**Why red blood cells are the exception:** because RBCs *don't* carry self markers, we can
**transfuse blood** (with cross-typing and matching). If RBCs were labeled "self," every
transfusion would be rejected. *(Transcript)*

**When do infections win?** Only when the body is **overwhelmed** — high stress, a second
("super") infection on top of a first, cancer, or immunosuppression (e.g., chemotherapy).
*(Transcript)*

> 📷 See **Slide 6** — the body being surveyed by WBCs; self markers → no destruction,
> non-self markers → detection → destruction.

## 3. MHC — the "self ID badge" system 🎯

**MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex)** = a **set of genes** that codes for cell-surface
receptors — a series of **glycoproteins** found on **all cells except red blood cells**. *(Slides 5, 25)*

The professor covers **only Class I and Class II** (there are four; the other two are "a
physiologist's job"). *(Transcript)*

| | **MHC Class I** | **MHC Class II** |
|---|---|---|
| What it is | DNA/genes that code **self markers** | Found on **Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs)** |
| Job | Labels **every** cell (except RBCs) as "self" | **Presents a piece of a destroyed invader** to the T4 helper |
| Where | On **every** nucleated cell | On APCs: **macrophages, monocytes, dendritic cells, B lymphocytes, natural killer cells** |
| Result | Cell recognized as "us" → left alone | Alerts the T4 helper → **turns on the immune response** |

**How an APC works (Class II):** an APC engulfs and destroys an invader, then **places a piece
of it on its own surface**. A **T4 helper** cell docks onto that display, "reads" it, and now
**knows** the threat is present — so it turns on the rest of the immune system. *(Transcript, Slides 23–29)*

> 📷 **Slide 27** — MHC Class I vs. Class II molecules and which cells carry them.
> 📷 **Slides 23 & 26** — activation of the CD4 / T-helper cell by an APC.

> **Plain-language:** MHC Class I is the ID badge every employee wears. MHC Class II is a
> security guard holding up a "wanted" poster (a piece of the intruder) so the chief (T4 helper)
> can sound the alarm.

## 4. The two immune responses 🎯

The immune system splits into two arms, based on the cells that run them. *(Slides 10, 36–37)*

| | **Humoral (Antibody-Mediated)** | **Cell-Mediated** |
|---|---|---|
| Run by | **B lymphocytes** (B cells) | **T lymphocytes** (T cells) |
| Weapon | **Antibodies** circulating in serum | Direct **cell-to-cell contact** + soluble products (**cytokines**) |
| Best against | **Extracellular** pathogens: bacteria, toxins, circulating viruses | **Intracellular** pathogens: viruses, fungi, intracellular bacteria, **tumor cells, transplants/grafts** |

**Where the cells come from (and how they're named):** all start as **stem cells in the bone
marrow**. *(Slides 11, 14; Transcript)*
- Mature in the **B**one marrow → **B lymphocytes** → make antibodies (humoral).
- Travel to the **T**hymus to specialize → **T lymphocytes** → cell-mediated.

> **History note (not tested):** B cells were first thought to be "**bursa**-derived" (an old
> theory). Turned out to be **bone-marrow** derived — and we got lucky that both start with "B,"
> so the name still works. *(Transcript)*

**Immunoprivileged sites** — a few tissues can be grafted without immune rejection because they
don't contact the blood: **upper layers of skin, the lens and cornea of the eye**. Every other
graft/transplant requires lifelong **immunosuppressants**. *(Transcript)*

> 📷 **Slide 11** — bone marrow → B cell; bone marrow → thymus → T cell.

## 5. Key definitions 🎯

- **Antigen (Ag)** — anything foreign (non-self) that triggers an immune response, e.g., an
  infectious agent. **Non-self = antigen.** *(Slide 8)*
- **Antibody (Ab)** — a **protein** made by the immune system that **binds and inactivates**
  non-self entities. Also called an **immunoglobulin (Ig)**. *(Slides 9, 41)*
- **Epitope** — the specific **marker on the surface** of an antigen that an antibody binds to.
  ("Be sure you know the word epitope — it's important." — professor) *(Transcript)*
- **Complement** — a group of **serum proteins** that attack and inactivate non-self entities. *(Slide 9)*
- **Titer** — a lab measure of how many antibodies you have. **Higher titer = more antibodies /
  more recent exposure.** Used to prove immunity (e.g., for nursing/dental/surg-tech program
  entry). *(Transcript)*

> **Antibody shape:** every antibody has a **"Y" shape**. The tips of the Y bind the epitopes.
> Bigger antibodies (like IgM) are several Y's joined together.

## 6. White blood cells you must know 🎯

The professor says: **don't** memorize staining or every cell's lifespan. **Do** know these roles. *(Slides 18–20; Transcript)*

**Two WBC families:**
- **Granulocytes** (visible granules): **neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils**
- **Agranulocytes**: **lymphocytes, monocytes/macrophages**

**The must-know jobs:**

| Cell | Shows up for… | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Neutrophils** | **Bacterial** infections | Phagocytize; do **diapedesis**; make **NETs** |
| **Eosinophils** | **Parasites** (worms) & **allergies** | Phagocytize; do **diapedesis**; attack big parasites from the outside |
| **Lymphocytes** | **Viral** infections | T cells and B cells |

**Diapedesis** 🎯 = when WBCs **squeeze out of the blood vessels and migrate into the tissues**
to fight infection where it's happening (e.g., into the lungs). *(Transcript)*

**Lab clue (differential WBC count)** *(Slide 20)*:
- ↑ **Eosinophils** → allergies or parasitic worms
- ↑ **Neutrophils / leukocytes** → bacterial disease
- ↑ **Lymphocytes** → viral infection

## 7. T lymphocytes (cell-mediated) 🎯

Three T cells to know *(Slides 15–17)*:

| T cell | Role | One-word |
|---|---|---|
| **T helper (T4 / CD4)** | Recognizes the threat and **turns on** the immune response | **Recognition** |
| **T cytolytic (cytotoxic)** | Destroys/inactivates infected cells | **Inactivation** |
| **T suppressor** | Regulates & modifies the response — ramps T cells up during infection, brings numbers **down** afterward | **Regulation** |

**The T4 / CD4 helper is the single most important cell** — the **sentinel** of the immune
system. It's the **only** cell that can **turn on** the immune response. *(Slide 16; Transcript)*

> **Why HIV/AIDS is so dangerous:** HIV attacks the **CD4 helper** — the one cell that switches
> the immune response on. Destroy enough of them and the whole system can't be activated, even
> though every other part is intact. The person becomes immunocompromised and dies from
> infections a healthy person would shrug off. *(Transcript)* **CD4 is "HIV's primary target."** *(Slide 12)*

> **Note:** **T4 = CD4.** The professor says "T4 helper"; the slides say "CD4." Same cell.

## 8. Phagocytosis — the 5 steps 🎯

**Mnemonic: CAKE** *(Slide 24; Transcript)*

| Step | | What happens |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | **Chemotaxis** | The phagocyte is **drawn toward** the microbe (attracted by its non-self markers) |
| **A** | **Adherence** | The phagocyte **latches on** to the microbe (also called attachment) |
| **K→I** | **Ingestion** | The microbe is pulled in by **endocytosis** into a **phagosome** |
| **K** | **Killing** | Phagosome + **lysosome** (digestive enzymes) fuse into a **phagolysosome**; the microbe is digested |
| **E** | **Elimination** | Leftover pieces are dumped out, cleaned by the kidneys, some excreted in urine |

> **Wait — CAKE = Chemotaxis, Adherence, (I)ngestion, Killing, Elimination.** The professor's
> official 5-stage list *(Slide 24)* is **Chemotaxis, Adherence, Ingestion, Killing, Elimination**.
> The "CAKE" mnemonic maps to the **first letters** he emphasizes; think **C-A-[i]-K-E**. (Some
> versions use "Ingestion" for the **I**.) Learn the five words in order — that's what's tested.

**Tie-in to MHC:** sometimes after killing, the phagocyte **displays a piece** of the microbe on
its surface = it becomes an **Antigen-Presenting Cell (MHC Class II)** → alerts the T4 helper. *(Transcript)*

> 📷 **Slides 23–29** — APC / phagocytosis / T-helper activation diagrams.

## 9. Killing big parasites — ADCC 🎯 (know this image)

Some invaders (parasitic **worms/helminths**) are **too big to engulf**, so the immune system
attacks them **from the outside**. *(Slides 30–33; Transcript)*

**Eosinophils and macrophages** attach to the parasite's surface and release weapons — the
professor wants you to know this **image** (Slides 29–31):

- 🔵 **Cytotoxic cytokines** (blue) — signal/recruit more eosinophils and macrophages
- 🔴 **Lytic enzymes** (red) — break down the parasite's outer surface
- 🟡 **Perforin enzymes** (yellow) — punch **holes** in the worm's surface

**Eosinophilia** (elevated eosinophils) is a classic sign of a **helminth (worm) infestation**. *(Slide 30)*

> **Real example from lecture:** *elephantiasis* — massive tissue swelling in the lower body
> caused by parasitic worms blocking fluid drainage, with eosinophils flooding the area.
> *(Transcript — the worm is [Wuchereria bancrofti]; see transcript flags.)*

**NETs — Neutrophil Extracellular Traps** 🎯 *(Slide 33; Transcript)*: neutrophils spin out a
**web of extracellular fibers** (like "spider webs") that **trap and kill** microbes — a
**non-phagocytic** killing method.

> 📷 **Slide 31** — eosinophil/macrophage attacking a large parasite (the key image).

## 10. Complement 🎯 (keep it simple — this is all he wants)

The professor: *"I'm not going to go any further into complement… it's very complicated, but I
just want you to know…"* *(Transcript, Slides 35, 51)*

**Complement = a group of proteins produced in a cascade.** It does **three** things:
1. **Lyses** microorganisms (bursts them open)
2. **Neutralizes** toxins and viruses
3. **Enhances phagocytosis**

**That's the testable version.** The extra detail below is context only:

- **"Complements"** (boosts) the work of antibodies and phagocytes; also promotes **inflammation**. *(Slide 51)*
- **MAC — Membrane Attack Complex** *(Slide 52)*: complement proteins that form **pores** in a
  pathogen's membrane, bursting the cell. *(📷 Slide 53)*
- **Your own cells survive** the cascade because they carry membrane-bound proteins that break
  down activated complement. *(Slide 35)*
- **Three activation pathways** *(Slide 50, 📷 Slide 55)* — reference only:
  - **Classical** — triggered by a pathogen directly, or by an **antibody** bound to it
  - **Lectin** — triggered by **mannose** on the pathogen surface
  - **Alternative** — triggered by a pathogen or injured tissue; **amplifies** the other two

> **Plain-language:** complement is a chain reaction of proteins (like dominoes). The finished
> product either drills holes in the invader (MAC), coats it so phagocytes grab it (opsonization),
> or calls in inflammation.

## 11. Cytokines 🎯

**Cytokines** = molecules (often secreted by immune cells that just met a pathogen) that
**activate and recruit more immune cells**, increasing the system's response. *(Slides 39, 40; Transcript)*

They are critical to **both innate and adaptive** immunity. Think of them as the immune system's
**text messages** — "threat here, send backup." *(Slide 39)*

## 12. B lymphocytes & the 5 antibodies 🎯

**B lymphocytes** form in response to a foreign entity and **secrete plasma cells, which make
immunoglobulins (antibodies)** against toxins, bacteria, viruses, and fungi. *(Slides 40, 41)*

**Mnemonic: MADGE** (the 5 antibody classes) — Ig **M, A, D, G, E** *(Slide 41; Transcript)*
> The professor also says the word **"MATCH"** to remember they all match a specific target, and
> **"MADGE"** for the five letters. Greek names: **Mu (M), Gamma (G), Epsilon (E), Alpha (A),
> Delta (D).**

**Key fact on each:** *(Slides 42–46)*

| Ig | Nickname / key fact | Job |
|---|---|---|
| **IgM** | The **"early bird"** — first to show up; **too big to cross the placenta**; short half-life (~5 days); pentamer (5 Y's) | Lyses microbes; neutralizes toxins/viruses; **agglutinates** (clumps) microbes |
| **IgG** | **Most important & most abundant (~80%)**; **"IgG is for life"**; **small → crosses the placenta** to give the fetus mom's immunity temporarily | Does everything IgM does + enhances phagocytosis; long-lasting memory |
| **IgA** | Found in **mucous membranes & secretions** (saliva, tears, breast milk) | Same jobs as IgM/IgG, but guards body surfaces |
| **IgD** | On the **surface of B lymphocytes**; <1%; role not fully understood | Helps **initiate** the immune response (B-cell receptor) |
| **IgE** | Very rare (~0.002%); the **allergy & parasite** antibody | Triggers **mast cells → histamine** → allergic symptoms; can cause **anaphylaxis** |

> **Anaphylaxis** *(Transcript)* = a **severe allergic reaction**: bronchioles constrict, blood
> vessels constrict → **hypoxia** (not enough oxygen). Can be fatal within minutes. Treated with
> an **EpiPen**. IgE-driven (e.g., peanuts, bee/ant stings).

> **⛔ NOT TESTED:** the numeric comparison chart (Slide 47) — molecular weights, exact
> percentages, half-lives. Know the **jobs and nicknames** above, not the numbers.

## 13. Primary vs. Secondary response 🎯

*(Slide 48)*

**Primary response** — the **first** time you meet an antigen (real infection **or** a vaccine —
your body can't tell the difference):
- **IgM appears first** (early), then fades.
- **IgG** rises and **stays for life**.

**Secondary response = "anamnestic response"** — a **later** re-exposure to the **same** antigen:
- IgM shows up again but is **overshadowed** by a **huge, fast burst of IgG** (because of **memory**).
- This large IgG surge = you are **immune**.

> **"Immune" does not mean you'll never get infected again.** It means a re-infection will be
> **less severe and shorter**. *(Transcript)*

**Why vaccines cause mild soreness/fever:** the vaccine is a **biological** with proteins on it;
your immune system makes antibodies against them. **Side effects (soreness, low fever, ache) =
the immune system working normally.** *(Transcript)*
- **Side effect** ≠ **adverse reaction.** Hives, shortness of breath, blood clots, anaphylaxis,
  Guillain-Barré = **adverse reactions**, which are different and rare. *(Transcript)*

> 📷 **Slide 48** — the primary vs. secondary antibody curve. Learn the shape: small IgM-led
> first bump, then a tall IgG-dominated second bump.

## 14. Immunization & Acquired Immunity 🎯

For **acquired immunity**, the immune system must recognize non-self, the antigen must be
**specific**, and the body must be able to **develop memory**. You can acquire it four ways
— **natural or artificial**, each **active or passive**: *(Transcript)*

| | **Active** — *your body makes the antibodies* (long-term) | **Passive** — *you receive ready-made antibodies* (short-term) |
|---|---|---|
| **Natural** | You **catch the infection** → body makes IgG (**for life**) | Antibodies from **mom**: across the **placenta** + in **colostrum / breast milk** |
| **Artificial** | A **vaccine** → body makes its own antibodies (long-term) | **Gamma globulin, convalescent plasma, antivenom/antitoxin** (~6 wks–3 mo) |

> **Memory hook:** **Active** = you did the *work* (lasts). **Passive** = you got a *loan* of
> antibodies (temporary). **Natural** = through life; **Artificial** = through medicine.

**Passive-immunity agents** *(all short-term; all IgG)*:
- **Gamma globulin** — pooled, concentrated IgG from **thousands of donors**; works best
  **regionally** (matches local pathogens); given IM or IV; lasts **~6 weeks–3 months**.
- **Convalescent plasma** — plasma from **recovered patients** (e.g., COVID) carrying IgG against
  that pathogen; cross-typed and matched before giving.
- **Antivenom / antitoxin (antisera)** — venom/toxin is injected into **sheep or goats**, which
  make IgG; their filtered plasma is given to the patient. It's **IgG against the venom** (e.g., snakebite).

### Vaccines

**Attenuated vs. inactivated** *(Salk vs. Sabin polio)*:
- **Live-attenuated** = the microbe is **weakened but still alive** — **Sabin** polio. Acts like
  the real microbe without causing disease. **Risk: reversion to virulence.**
- **Inactivated** = the microbe is **dead/inactive** — **Salk** polio. Safe; can't revert.

**🎯 The 5 vaccine platforms** — *"be sure you know the differences… I'm sure you're going to
see something about them on the exam."*
1. **mRNA / DNA** — nucleic acid teaches your cells to make a protein (e.g., the spike); you make
   antibodies against it. *(Pfizer/Moderna COVID.)*
2. **Live-attenuated** — a **weakened whole** microbe (usually a virus). *(Sabin.)*
3. **Inactivated** — a **killed/inactive whole** microbe. *(Salk.)*
4. **Subunit** — only a **piece** of the microbe (e.g., a protein or flagella) — enough to trigger immunity.
5. **Viral vector** — a **harmless virus** (e.g., adenovirus) delivers the genetic instructions. *(J&J, Ebola.)*

**🎯 Adjuvant** — something **added to a vaccine** (**usually a salt**) that causes mild local
**irritation** → draws in more white blood cells → a **stronger immune response**.

**A good vaccine should:** (1) have **low toxicity** / few adverse reactions; (2) protect against
the **natural (wild) pathogen**; (3) stimulate **both B-cell (humoral) and T-cell (cell-mediated)**
responses; (4) produce **long-term memory**; (5) need **few boosters**; (6) be **inexpensive**;
(7) have a **long shelf life** and be **easy to give**.

**Herd immunity** — if roughly **70–80%** of a population is immune, the pathogen can't spread, so
even the unvaccinated are protected. *(Borrowed from ranchers vaccinating ~70–80% of cattle against
anthrax.)*

**Thimerosal** — a **mercury-containing preservative** that **stabilizes the proteins** in
**multi-dose vials**. The Wakefield MMR-autism claim was a **fraudulent, retracted study** (only 8
hand-picked patients); large studies show **no link** between vaccines/thimerosal and autism. It's
been out of vaccines for **20+ years**; **single-dose** syringes never contained it.

> **Nosode** *(not recommended — dangerous)* — consuming or injecting body secretions/pustule
> exudate that contain the **live, fully-virulent** agent to try to build immunity. Far more
> dangerous than a vaccine (you're exposed to the disease in full force).

> **Side effects vs. adverse reactions (recap):** soreness, mild fever, runny nose = the immune
> system **working normally**. Hives, shortness of breath, **anaphylaxis** = **adverse reactions** (rare, different).

## 15. The immune response in the very young

Newborns lack a developed **adaptive** (memory) immune response, so they rely on the
**cell-mediated** response and on **passive** antibodies from mom. This is why a young child's
white-blood-cell count can spike dramatically during infection *(e.g., whooping cough)*. *(Transcript)*
