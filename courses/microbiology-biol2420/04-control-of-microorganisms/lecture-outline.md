## 1. The goal of microbial control 🎯
- To control microbes you either **kill** them or **inhibit** their growth. *(Slide 2)*
- **Microbial death** = irreversible cell damage + inability to reproduce; death proceeds
  **logarithmically** past a threshold dose. *(Slide 3)*

## 2. Terminology of microbial control 🎯 (Table 9.1)
- **Sterilization** (all microbes) · **Disinfection** (most, nonliving surface) · **Antisepsis**
  (living tissue) · **Degerming** (mechanical removal) · **Sanitization** (safe public-health
  levels) · **Pasteurization** (heat for foods) · **Aseptic** (contaminant-free). *(Slide 4)*
- Suffixes: **-cide/-cidal = kills**; **-stasis/-static = inhibits.**
- "Sterile" is absolute — there's no "almost sterile."

## 3. The germicidal spectrum 🎯
- **Cleaner → Sanitizer → Disinfectant → Virucide → Sterilant.** Only the **sterilant** eliminates
  **spores**. *(Slide 5)*

## 4. Classifying the methods 🎯
- **Age-old methods** (osmotic pressure, refrigeration) **suppress but don't kill**; modern
  **physical** and **chemical** methods **kill**. *(Slide 6)*
- **Figure 11.1** — master map: Physical / Chemical / Mechanical → sterilization, disinfection,
  antisepsis, decontamination. *(Slide 7)*
- The three categories: **Physical** (force), **Chemical** (chemicals), **Mechanical** (removal).
  *(Slide 8)*

## 5. How antimicrobial agents work 🎯
- **Alter cell walls & membranes** → osmotic bursting / leakage / loss of viral envelope. *(Slide 9)*
- **Damage proteins & nucleic acids** → denature proteins, alter DNA/RNA, fatal mutants, halt
  synthesis. *(Slides 10–11)*
- **Ideal agent:** cheap, fast, stable, controls all microbes while harmless to us. *(Slide 12)*

## 6. Factors affecting efficacy 🎯
- **Site to treat** — can't use harsh heat/chemicals on people or fragile objects. *(Slide 13)*
- **Number, nature, temperature/pH, concentration, mechanism, organic matter/inhibitors.** *(Slide 14)*
- **Germicide levels:** **high** (incl. endospores), **intermediate** (not endospores), **low**
  (vegetative only). *(Slide 15)*
- **Phenol coefficient** — killing power as a ratio to phenol; >1.0 = better; older method. ⛔ the
  math itself. *(Slide 16)*

## 7. Physical control overview 🎯
- Overview slides: autoclave is the **preferred sterilization** method; **121 °C at 15 psi** kills
  all but **prions**; also filters, low temperatures, desiccation, osmotic pressure, radiation, dry
  heat, pasteurization. *(Slides 17–18)*

## 8. Heat 🎯
- **Higher temp = microbicidal; lower temp = microbistatic.** Moist vs. dry heat. *(Slide 19)*
- **TDT** (shortest time at a temp) vs. **TDP** (lowest temp to kill in 10 min); higher temp →
  shorter time. *(Slide 21)* ⛔ Table 9.4 exact cells. *(Slides 20, 28)*

### Moist heat
- **Autoclave — steam under pressure:** 15 psi → 121 °C, ~15 min; kills everything but prions.
  *(Slide 22)* Pressure–temperature graph. *(Slide 23, Figure 9.5)*
- **Pasteurization:** flash **71.6 °C/15 s**, batch **63–66 °C/30 min**; **does not kill endospores
  or thermoduric microbes.** *(Slide 24)* Milk table. *(Slide 25, Table 9.2)* ⛔ exact cells.
- **Boiling water:** disinfection **not** sterilization; ~30 min. *(Slide 26)*

### Dry heat
- **Hot-air oven:** 150–180 °C for 2–4 h; for heat-resistant items; **effective against endotoxin.**
  Incineration destroys everything. *(Slide 27)*

## 9. Radiation 🎯
- Radiation = high-velocity atomic energy; for control: **gamma, X-ray, UV.** *(Slide 29)*
- Uses: **food and medical products.** *(Slide 30)*
- **UV:** ~100–400 nm; **germicidal lamp 254 nm**; **not penetrating**; destroys fungi/spores,
  bacteria, protozoa, viruses. *(Slide 31)*
- **UV uses:** usually **disinfection** — hospital/operating rooms, schools, food prep, dental;
  water treatment. *(Slide 32)*
- Ionizing (deep, sterilizes) vs. nonionizing/UV (shallow, disinfects).

## 10. Low temperature, drying, osmosis 🎯 (microbistatic)
- **Refrigeration/freezing:** slows metabolism; **halts but doesn't kill**; psychrophiles still
  grow; slow freezing more damaging than quick. *(Slide 33)*
- **Desiccation** = drying, microbiostatic only; **lyophilization** (freeze-drying) preserves
  cultures long-term without ice-crystal damage. *(Slide 34)*
- **Osmotic pressure:** salt/sugar → hypertonic → cells desiccate; **fungi tolerate it better than
  bacteria.** *(Slide 35)*

## 11. Chemical control — overview 🎯
- Splits into **gas sterilants** (ethylene oxide) and **antiseptics/disinfectants** (germicidal
  chemicals). *(Slides 36–37)*
- ~10,000 chemicals made, ~1,000 routine; liquids/gases/solids; **tincture** = dissolved in alcohol.
  *(Slide 38)*
- ⛔ Table 11.4 (required concentrations/times per microbe). *(Slide 39)*
- **Table 9.5** — the agent families + levels. *(Slide 40)*
- Agents target walls/membranes/proteins/DNA; effect varies with temp, time, organic matter.
  *(Slide 41)*

## 12. The chemical agent families 🎯 (Slides 42–52)
- **Phenol & phenolics** — denature/disrupt; work around organic matter; **Lysol, triclosan**;
  odor. *(Slide 42)*
- **Alcohols** — intermediate; **70% ethanol swab**; evaporate fast. *(Slide 43)*
- **Halogens** — iodine, iodophors (**Betadine**), chlorine/**bleach**, chloramines, bromine.
  *(Slide 44)*
- **Chlorhexidine** — chlorine + two phenolic rings; bactericidal (gram + and –) but **not spores**;
  mild, rapid. *(Slide 45)*
- **Oxidizing agents** — peroxides, ozone, peracetic acid; **high-level**; H₂O₂ (catalase
  neutralizes → not for open wounds); **peracetic acid = sporocide.** *(Slide 46)*
- **Surfactants** — soaps (**degerming, not antimicrobial**); detergents; **quats** = low-level
  disinfectants. *(Slide 47)* Detergent mode: disrupts membranes; **not endospores.** *(Slide 48)*
- **Heavy metals** — alter protein 3-D shape; low-level; **1% silver nitrate** (newborn eyes,
  N. gonorrhoeae), **thimerosal** (vaccine preservative), **copper** (algae). *(Slide 49)*
- **Aldehydes** — **glutaraldehyde** disinfects (short) & sterilizes (long); **formalin** embalms.
  *(Slide 50)*
- **Gaseous agents** — **ethylene oxide**, propylene oxide, β-propiolactone; closed-chamber
  sterilization of heat/moisture-sensitive items; hazardous. *(Slide 51)*
- **Antimicrobials** — antibiotics + synthetics; mostly treat disease. *(Slide 52)*

## 13. Resistance & safety 🎯
- **Overuse of antiseptics/disinfectants promotes resistant microbes**; little evidence extra use
  improves health. *(Slide 53)*
- Everyday products mapped to families (Lysol/Clorox wipes, triclosan soaps, ethanol sanitizer).
  *(Slide 54, Table 11.5)*

## 14. Mechanical control 🎯
- **Air/environmental control** — 21 CFR 211.46: ventilation, air filtration, heating/cooling;
  prefilters + particulate filters. *(Slide 55)*
- **Removal of bacteria** — dust is a carrier; filtration; **directional airflow/dilution**;
  cleaning SOPs. *(Slide 56)*
- **Hand hygiene** — WHO; degerming; **Grimebusters** commonly-missed spots (fingertips, nails,
  thumbs, between fingers, wrists, backs of hands). *(Slides 57–58)*
- **HEPA filters** remove airborne bacteria & viruses (cabin air, OR). *(Slide 59)*

## 15. Filtration 🎯
- **Removes microbes from air and liquids**; fluid passes, microbes too big don't. *(Slide 60)*
- **Membrane filters** — cellulose acetate/polycarbonate/plastic; controlled pore size.
- Uses: heat-sensitive liquids, beverage decontamination, **water purification**, **HEPA** air.
  *(Slide 61)*
- Filtration apparatus. *(Slide 62, Figure 9.9)*
- **Pore-size table** — smaller pore catches smaller microbes; **0.22 µm** = bacteria-sterilizing
  filter; need smaller pores for viruses. *(Slide 63, Table 9.3)* ⛔ exact numbers.

---

## The takeaway 2×2
- **Physical / Chemical / Mechanical** × **kills (-cide) / inhibits (-static).**
- **Endospores** are the hardest to kill — only **high-level / sterilizing** methods reach them;
  anything that merely "disinfects" leaves spores alive.
