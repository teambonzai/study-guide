## 1. Why we control microorganisms — the two goals 🎯

To control microbes you do exactly one of two things: *(Slide 2)*

1. **Kill them**, or
2. **Inhibit their growth.**

That single choice drives every term in this lecture. Anything ending in **-cide** *kills*;
anything ending in **-static** *inhibits* (stops growth without killing).

**What "microbial death" actually means** *(Slide 3)*:
- Cell structures become **dysfunctional** and the cell takes **irreversible damage**.
- The cell can **no longer reproduce**, even under ideal conditions.
- Death starts once a **threshold** of the killing agent is reached, then proceeds in a
  **logarithmic** (steady-percentage) manner — a population dies off in a curve, not all at once.

> **Why (logarithmic death):** a given dose kills a fixed **percentage** each interval, not a fixed
> number. So the last few cells take proportionally the longest to kill — which is exactly why
> sterilization needs a real margin of time.

## 2. Terminology — the words that are the test 🎯

This is the highest-yield slide in the deck *(Table 9.1, Slide 4)*. Learn each term and one example.

| Term | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Sterilization** | Destroy/remove **ALL** microorganisms and viruses, in or on an object | Steam under pressure, incineration, ethylene oxide gas; canning |
| **Disinfection** | Destroy **most** microbes and viruses on a **nonliving** surface | Phenolics, alcohols, aldehydes on tabletops |
| **Antisepsis** | Reduce the number of microbes on **living tissue** | Iodine or alcohol on skin |
| **Degerming** | **Mechanical** removal of microbes from a surface | Hand washing; alcohol swab before an injection |
| **Sanitization** | Reduce microbes to **safe public-health levels** | Washing dishes in scalding water in restaurants |
| **Pasteurization** | Use **heat** to destroy pathogens & reduce spoilage organisms in foods/beverages | Pasteurized milk and fruit juice |
| **Aseptic** | An environment or procedure **free of contaminants** | Surgical field prep; sterile technique |
| **-cide / -cidal** | Suffix meaning it **kills** a type of microbe | Bacteri**cide**, fungi**cide**, viru**cide** |
| **-stasis / -static** | Suffix meaning it **inhibits** (not kills) | Bacterio**static**, fungi**static** |

> **Important:** **"Sterile" is absolute** — there is no such thing as "almost sterile" or "mostly
> sterile." An object is either sterile (zero viable microbes) or it is not. Disinfection and
> sanitization only *reduce* the load.

> **Analogy:** think of a kitchen. **Cleaning** wipes off the visible gunk. **Sanitizing** knocks
> the germ count down to a safe level (dishwasher). **Disinfecting** kills most of what's left on
> the counter. **Sterilizing** is the surgical-tray standard — nothing alive survives, spores
> included.

**The germicidal spectrum** *(Slide 5 — know the order)*:

**Cleaner → Sanitizer → Disinfectant → Virucide → Sterilant**
- **Cleaner** — aids in **soil (dirt) removal**; doesn't necessarily kill anything.
- **Sanitizer** — **reduces the number** of bacteria to safe levels.
- **Disinfectant** — **kills fungi, bacteria, and viruses** on surfaces.
- **Virucide** — **kills viruses** specifically.
- **Sterilant** — **eliminates ALL** fungi, bacteria, viruses, **and spores.**

> 📷 See **Slide 5** — the color-coded spectrum from "aids in soil removal" up to "eliminates all
> microbial life including spores."

## 3. The three categories of control 🎯

Every method in this lecture is one of three kinds *(Slide 8)*:

| Category | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Physical control** | A **physical force** is exerted on the microbes (heat, radiation, cold, pressure) |
| **Chemical control** | **Chemicals** are used to kill or inhibit the microbes |
| **Mechanical control** | The microbes are **physically removed** from the environment (filtration) |

There's also an "**age-old methods**" idea *(Slide 6)*: **osmotic pressure** (salting/sugaring foods)
and **refrigeration** are ancient tricks that **only suppress** growth — they **don't kill**. The
"better, quality-assured" methods that actually **kill** microbes are the modern physical and
chemical ones.

> 📷 **Slide 7 (Figure 11.1)** — the master map of the whole lecture. **Physical agents** (heat →
> dry/moist; radiation → ionizing/nonionizing), **Chemical agents** (gases; liquids on animate vs.
> inanimate surfaces), and **Mechanical removal** (filtration of air & liquids), each ending in
> **sterilization**, **disinfection**, **antisepsis**, or **decontamination**. Learn which branch
> reaches "sterilization" and which only reaches "disinfection."

**Four outcome words from that map** *(Slide 7)*:
- **Sterilization** — complete removal/destruction of **all** viable microbes (inanimate objects).
- **Disinfection** — destroys/removes **vegetative pathogens but NOT endospores**; inanimate objects.
- **Antisepsis** — chemicals applied to **body surfaces** to destroy/inhibit vegetative pathogens.
- **Decontamination** — the **mechanical removal** of most microbes.

## 4. How antimicrobial agents actually work 🎯

Physical and chemical agents damage microbes in a few shared ways *(Slides 9–11)*:

**A. Alter cell walls and membranes** *(Slide 9)*
- The **cell wall** keeps the cell's integrity; disrupt it and the cell **bursts from osmotic
  pressure**.
- The **cytoplasmic membrane** controls what goes in/out; damage it and **cell contents leak out**.
- A **viral envelope** is needed for the virus to attach to its target cell; damage it and the
  virus **can't infect**.

**B. Damage proteins and nucleic acids** *(Slides 10–11)*
- Protein function depends on **3-D shape**; **extreme heat or chemicals denature** proteins
  (unfold them → they stop working).
- Chemicals, radiation, and heat can **alter or destroy DNA/RNA** → can create **fatal mutants** or
  **halt protein synthesis**.
- Specific hits: **binding ribosomes** to stop translation; **binding DNA** irreversibly to block
  transcription/translation; acting as **mutagens**.

> **Why this matters:** almost every method below works through **one of these**: pop the
> wall/membrane, or wreck the proteins/nucleic acids. When you meet a new agent, ask "does it
> denature protein, dissolve membranes, or attack DNA?" — that's the whole game.

**The ideal agent would be** *(Slide 12)*: **inexpensive, fast-acting, stable in storage,** and able
to **control all microbial growth while staying harmless** to humans, animals, and objects. (No real
agent hits all of these at once — hence trade-offs.)

## 5. Factors that change how well a method works 🎯

The same disinfectant can work or fail depending on conditions *(Slides 13–14)*:

- **Site to be treated** — harsh chemicals and extreme heat **can't** be used on people, animals, or
  fragile objects; the method is chosen to match the **site** of the procedure.
- **Number of microbes** — more organisms → longer to kill them all.
- **Nature of the microbes** — some are far tougher (endospores!).
- **Temperature and pH** of the environment.
- **Concentration** (or dose) of the agent.
- **Mechanism of action** of the agent.
- **Presence of organic matter, solvents, and inhibitors** — blood, feces, and dirt **shield**
  microbes and neutralize many disinfectants.

**Relative susceptibility → germicide levels** *(Slide 15)* 🎯:

| Level | Kills | 
|---|---|
| **High-level** | **All pathogens, including endospores** (essentially sterilizing) |
| **Intermediate-level** | Fungal spores, protozoan cysts, viruses, and pathogenic bacteria — **but not endospores** |
| **Low-level** | Vegetative bacteria, fungi, protozoa, and **some** viruses |

> **Memory hook (hardest → easiest to kill):** **prions → endospores → protozoan cysts →
> mycobacteria (waxy) → naked viruses → vegetative bacteria/fungi → enveloped viruses.** Only a
> **high-level / sterilizing** method kills the top of that list.

**Evaluating a disinfectant — the phenol coefficient** *(Slide 16)*:
- The **phenol coefficient** rates an agent's killing power **as a ratio to phenol**.
- **> 1.0** = **more** effective than phenol; **< 1.0** = **less** effective.
- It's an **older method**, now largely **replaced** by newer tests. *(⛔ the arithmetic itself is
  reference-only — just know what the ratio means.)*

## 6. Heat — the most important physical method 🎯

**General rule:** **higher temperatures are microbi*cidal* (kill); lower temperatures are
microbi*static* (inhibit).** Heat comes in **moist** and **dry** forms. *(Slide 19)*

**Time–temperature trade-off** *(Slide 21)*: **higher temperature → shorter exposure needed; lower
temperature → longer exposure needed.** Two definitions from this slide:
- **Thermal Death Time (TDT)** — the **shortest time** to kill all test microbes **at a given
  temperature**.
- **Thermal Death Point (TDP)** — the **lowest temperature** that kills all microbes in a sample
  **in 10 minutes**.

### Moist heat

Moist heat kills faster and at lower temperatures than dry heat (steam carries a lot of energy and
penetrates well).

- **Autoclave — steam under pressure** *(Slide 22)* 🎯 — the **preferred method of sterilization.**
  Pressure **raises the temperature** of steam. The standard cycle is **15 psi → 121 °C** (for ~15
  minutes) — the most efficient pressure/temperature combo for sterilization. **It kills
  everything except prions.**
  > **Watch a slide typo:** the physical-methods **overview slide (Slide 17)** writes the autoclave
  > as "**100 °C** under 1 atm OR 15 psi." That's wrong — plain steam at 1 atm is only 100 °C, but the
  > **pressurized** cycle that actually sterilizes is **121 °C at 15 psi** (confirmed on Slide 22 and
  > Table 9.4). Learn **121 °C.**
  > 📷 **Slide 23 (Figure 9.5)** — the graph of **pressure (psi) vs. temperature (°C)**: as pressure
  > rises, so does the steam temperature. That's *why* the autoclave works — plain boiling water
  > tops out at 100 °C, but pressurized steam reaches 121 °C.
- **Boiling water** *(Slide 26)* — **disinfection, NOT sterilization.** Boil for ~30 minutes; kills
  vegetative cells but **not endospores**.
- **Pasteurization** *(Slide 24)* — heats liquids enough to **kill pathogens and spoilage
  organisms while keeping flavor and nutrients.** Uses special heat exchangers.
  - **Flash method:** **71.6 °C for 15 seconds.**
  - **Batch method:** **63–66 °C for 30 minutes.**
  - **Does NOT kill endospores or thermoduric (heat-tolerant) microbes** — it's disinfection, not
    sterilization.
  > 📷 **Slide 25 (Table 9.2)** — milk treatments: batch 63 °C/30 min, flash 72 °C/15 s, ultra-high-
  > temperature pasteurization 134 °C/1 s, UHT sterilization 140 °C/1–3 s. *(⛔ exact cells are
  > reference — know flash vs. batch and that UHT can actually sterilize.)*

### Dry heat

Dry heat needs **higher temperatures and longer times** than moist heat *(Slide 27)*:
- **Hot-air oven** — an electric oven whose coils radiate heat; **150–180 °C for 2–4 hours.**
- Used for **heat-resistant items that don't sterilize well with moist heat** (powders, oils, metals).
- **Effective against endotoxin** — unlike most methods, prolonged dry heat can destroy the
  lipopolysaccharide **endotoxin** of gram-negative cell walls.
- **Incineration** (flaming, burning) is the ultimate dry heat — it **destroys everything completely.**

> 📷 **Slides 20 & 28 (Table 9.4)** — physical methods side by side: boiling (10 min/100 °C),
> autoclaving (15 min/121 °C), pasteurization (15 s/72 °C), UHT (1–3 s/140 °C), hot-air (2 h/171 °C),
> incineration; then the low-temperature and radiation rows. *(⛔ exact numbers are reference.)*

## 7. Radiation 🎯

**Radiation** = energy emitted by atomic activity, moving at high velocity through matter/space.
For microbial control the deck lists **gamma rays, X-rays, and ultraviolet (UV).** *(Slides 29–30)*

Two kinds, and the difference is the whole point:

| | **Ionizing** (gamma, X-ray, cathode) | **Nonionizing** (UV) |
|---|---|---|
| Energy | **High** — knocks electrons off atoms | **Lower** — excites but doesn't ionize |
| Penetration | **Deep** (through packaging, tissue) | **Shallow** — surface only |
| Effect | Destroys **DNA** → **sterilizes** | Forms **thymine dimers** in DNA → **disinfects** |
| Uses | **Food** and **medical products** (sterilizing single-use supplies) *(Slide 30)* | Air, surfaces, water |

**Ultraviolet (UV) detail** *(Slides 31–32)*:
- Wavelength **~100–400 nm**; the **germicidal lamp** works at **254 nm.**
- **Not as penetrating** as ionizing radiation (it can't get through solids or even glass well).
- Powerful for destroying **fungal cells and spores, bacterial vegetative cells, protozoa, and
  viruses.**
- Uses are usually **disinfection, not sterilization:** hospital and operating rooms, schools,
  food-prep areas, dental offices; **treating drinking water** and purifying liquids.

> **Why UV only disinfects:** it can't penetrate, so it only reaches microbes it directly shines on
> — shadowed or embedded organisms survive. Great for exposed air/surfaces, useless for the inside
> of an object.

## 8. Cold, drying & osmotic pressure — the microbi*static* methods 🎯

These **inhibit** growth; they generally **don't sterilize** — hence the "age-old methods" label.

**Low temperature / refrigeration & freezing** *(Slide 33)*:
- Cold **decreases microbial metabolism, growth, and reproduction** (chemical reactions run slower;
  in freezing, **liquid water isn't available**).
- **Refrigeration halts growth of most pathogens** but **doesn't kill** them.
- **Psychrophilic** microbes can **still multiply** in refrigerated food (why the fridge isn't
  forever).
- **Slow freezing is more damaging** to cells than quick freezing (ice crystals). Organisms **vary**
  in how well they survive freezing.

**Desiccation & lyophilization** *(Slide 34)*:
- **Desiccation** (drying) inhibits growth by **removing water** — it's **microbiostatic only**
  (many microbes survive dry and revive later).
- **Lyophilization** (freeze-drying) is used for **long-term preservation of microbial cultures** —
  it freezes then removes water under vacuum, **preventing damaging ice crystals.**

**Osmotic pressure** *(Slide 35)*:
- **High concentrations of salt or sugar** inhibit growth (classic food preservation — jerky, jam,
  salted fish).
- Cells placed in a **hypertonic** salt/sugar solution **lose water and desiccate.**
- **Fungi tolerate hypertonic environments better than bacteria** — which is why mold, not bacteria,
  grows on jam.

## 9. Chemical control — the agent families 🎯

Roughly **10,000** antimicrobial chemicals are manufactured; about **1,000** are used routinely in
health care and the home. They come as **liquids, gases, or solids.** A **tincture** is an agent
**dissolved in alcohol** (or a water–alcohol mix). *(Slide 38)*

Chemical agents attack the same targets as before — **cell walls, membranes, proteins, or DNA** —
and their effect **varies with temperature, exposure time, and how much organic matter** is present.
*(Slide 41)*

> 📷 **Slide 36/37** — chemical control splits into **gas sterilants** (e.g., ethylene oxide) and
> **antiseptics/disinfectants** (germicidal chemicals).

**The chemical families (Table 9.5, Slide 40)** 🎯 — target + level:

| Family | How it works | Level | Examples / uses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phenol (carbolic acid)** | Denatures proteins, disrupts membranes | Interm.–low | The original surgical antiseptic; now replaced by milder phenolics |
| **Phenolics** | Denature proteins, disrupt membranes | Interm.–low | **Lysol, triclosan**; work even with organic matter; disagreeable odor |
| **Alcohols** | Denature proteins, disrupt membranes | Intermediate | **70% ethanol** skin swab; also a solvent for tinctures; evaporates fast |
| **Halogens** (iodine, chlorine, bromine, fluorine) | Oxidize / denature enzymes | Intermediate | **Iodophors (Betadine), chlorine in drinking water, bleach, chloramines** in dressings, bromine in hot tubs |
| **Oxidizing agents** (peroxides, ozone, peracetic acid) | Oxidize microbial enzymes | **High** | **H₂O₂** disinfects & sterilizes surfaces; **ozone** for water; **peracetic acid** sterilizes equipment |
| **Surfactants** (soaps, detergents, quats) | Lower surface tension; disrupt membranes | Low | **Soaps = degerming** (not antimicrobial); **quats** = low-level disinfectants |
| **Heavy metals** (silver, copper, mercury, zinc) | Denature proteins (alter 3-D shape) | Low | **1% silver nitrate** for newborn eyes; **thimerosal** preserves vaccines; **copper** for algae |
| **Aldehydes** (glutaraldehyde, formaldehyde) | Cross-link & denature proteins/nucleic acids | **High** | **Glutaraldehyde** disinfects (short) & sterilizes (long); **formalin** embalms |
| **Gaseous agents** (ethylene oxide, propylene oxide, β-propiolactone) | Denature proteins/DNA by cross-linking | **High** | **Sterilize heat- & moisture-sensitive items**; hazardous, explosive, carcinogenic |
| **Enzymes** | Denature proteins | High vs. target | **Removal of prions** from instruments |
| **Antimicrobials** (antibiotics, synthetics) | Hit walls, membranes, synthesis, DNA | Interm.–low | Treatment of infectious disease |

Now the agents the deck spends its own slides on:

**Phenol & phenolics** *(Slide 42)* — intermediate-to-low; **denature proteins and disrupt
membranes**; stay **active even around organic matter** and last a long time; common in health care,
labs, and homes (**Lysol, triclosan**); downside is **odor and side effects.**

**Alcohols** *(Slide 43)* — intermediate; **denature proteins, disrupt membranes**; **evaporate
rapidly** (good = leaves no residue; bad = short contact time); **70% ethanol swab before an
injection.**

**Halogens** *(Slide 44)* — intermediate; thought to **damage enzymes by oxidation or denaturation**;
**iodine tablets, iodophors (Betadine), chlorinated drinking water, bleach, chloramine wound
dressings, bromine in hot tubs.**

**Chlorhexidine** *(Slide 45)* — a complex organic base with **chlorine and two phenolic rings**;
**targets cell membranes and protein structure**; at moderate–high concentration it's **bactericidal
for gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria but inactive against spores**; **mild, low toxicity,
rapid action** (common skin prep and mouthwash).

**Oxidizing agents** *(Slide 46)* — **peroxides, ozone, peracetic acid** kill by **oxidizing
microbial enzymes**; **high-level.** **Hydrogen peroxide** can disinfect and sterilize surfaces —
but **catalase in tissue neutralizes it**, so it's **not useful on open wounds.** **Ozone** treats
drinking water; **peracetic acid** is an effective **sporocide** for sterilizing equipment.

**Surfactants ("surface-active" agents)** *(Slides 47–48)* — reduce the **surface tension** of
solvents so they dissolve solutes better. **Soaps** have a hydrophilic and a hydrophobic end →
excellent **degerming** agents but **not antimicrobial** (they lift microbes off so they rinse away).
**Detergents** are positively charged; **quaternary ammonium compounds (quats)** are colorless,
tasteless, human-safe, and **antimicrobial** → ideal for medical/industrial use, but only
**low-level disinfectants.**
  > 📷 **Slide 48** — how detergents work: they **disrupt vital membrane function.** Effective
  > against **bacteria, fungi, mycobacteria, and viruses — but NOT endospores.**

**Heavy metals** *(Slide 49)* — ions are antimicrobial because they **alter the 3-D shape of
proteins**, shutting them down; **low-level, bacteriostatic/fungistatic.** **1% silver nitrate** in
newborns' eyes prevents **gonococcal (N. gonorrhoeae) blindness**; **thimerosal** preserves
vaccines; **copper** controls **algae** in reservoirs, tanks, and pools (it blocks chlorophyll).

**Aldehydes** *(Slide 50)* — compounds with terminal **–CHO groups**; they **cross-link** amino,
hydroxyl, sulfhydryl, and carboxyl groups to **denature proteins and inactivate nucleic acids.**
**Glutaraldehyde** both **disinfects (short exposure) and sterilizes (long exposure)**; **formalin**
(formaldehyde solution) is used in **embalming** and to disinfect rooms/instruments.

**Gaseous agents** *(Slide 51)* — **ethylene oxide, propylene oxide, beta-propiolactone** used in
**closed chambers** to **sterilize** items; they **denature proteins and DNA by cross-linking.**
Used in hospitals and dental offices for **heat- and moisture-sensitive equipment**, but they're
**hazardous — often explosive, extremely poisonous, and potentially carcinogenic.**

**Antimicrobials** *(Slide 52)* — antibiotics plus semi-synthetic and synthetic chemicals; mostly
used to **treat disease** inside the body, though some control microbes on the outside too.

## 10. Resistance & the safety caveat 🎯

**Overusing antiseptics and disinfectants promotes resistant microbes.** *(Slide 53)* There's
**little evidence** that heavy household use of antiseptic/disinfectant products improves human or
animal health, and their overuse **selects for resistant organisms** — the same logic as antibiotic
overuse.

> **Important:** more disinfectant is not automatically better. Using germicidal products
> everywhere, all the time, breeds tougher microbes without a matching health benefit.

> 📷 **Slide 54 (Table 11.5)** — active ingredients in everyday products (Lysol wipes, Clorox wipes,
> antibacterial soaps with triclosan, hand sanitizers with ethanol, etc.), mapped to their chemical
> family. Useful for connecting a household brand to its category.

## 11. Mechanical control — removing microbes 🎯

**Mechanical control physically removes microbes** rather than killing them *(Slides 8, 55–63)*.
The two big ideas are **air control** and **filtration.**

**Air & environmental control** *(Slides 55–56)*:
- Governed by regulations such as **21 CFR 211.46** (pharmaceutical manufacturing): **ventilation,
  air filtration, air heating and cooling** must give adequate control over microbes, dust, and
  humidity — including **prefilters and particulate air filters.**
- **Removing bacteria** from a space: **dust is a carrier**, so dust is controlled; **ambient
  bacteria are removed by filtration**; **directional airflow / air flushing (dilution)** controls
  internal spread; **surfaces are kept clean by strict cleaning SOPs.**

**Hand hygiene** *(Slides 57–58)* — the **WHO** stresses hand hygiene to prevent avoidable
health-care infections. Hand washing is a form of **degerming** (mechanical removal). The
"**Grimebusters**" slide flags the spots people **miss**: **fingertips, under the nails, thumbs,
between the fingers, wrists, and the backs of the hands.**
- **Technique** *(Slide 58 fine print)*: **alcohol-based hand rub (ABHR) is preferred** *unless hands
  are visibly soiled* — rub for **~15–20 seconds**; if soiled, **wash with soap, water, and friction
  for ≥30 seconds**; remove jewelry first.

**Filtration** *(Slides 59–63)* 🎯 — the key mechanical method:
- **Removes microbes from air and liquids** by straining fluid through a filter whose pores let
  **fluid** through but are **too small for microorganisms.** *(Slide 60)*
- **Membrane filters** are thin sheets of **cellulose acetate, polycarbonate,** or plastic, with
  **controlled, standardized pore sizes.**
- **Uses** *(Slide 61)*: prepare **heat-sensitive liquids** (that can't be autoclaved),
  **decontaminate beverages without changing flavor**, **purify water**, and remove **airborne
  contaminants** with **HEPA filters** (e.g., cabin air, operating rooms).
  > 📷 **Slide 62 (Figure 9.9)** — the membrane-filtration apparatus: nonsterile medium → membrane
  > filter → vacuum pump → **sterile** medium below, with an SEM of bacteria caught on the filter.

**Pore size vs. what's trapped** *(Table 9.3, Slide 63)* — know the **trend**, not the decimals:
the **smaller the pore, the smaller the microbe it can catch.**

| Pore size (µm) | Traps |
|---|---|
| 5 | Multicellular algae, animals, fungi |
| 3 | Yeasts and larger unicellular algae |
| 1.2 | Protozoa, small unicellular algae |
| 0.45 | Largest bacteria |
| 0.22 | Largest viruses and **most bacteria** (the classic "sterilizing" filter for solutions) |
| 0.025 | Larger viruses; pliable bacteria (**mycoplasmas, rickettsias, chlamydias,** some spirochetes) |
| 0.01 | Smallest viruses |

> ⛔ The exact numbers are reference. What's testable is the **idea**: a **0.22 µm** filter is the
> standard for **sterilizing heat-sensitive liquids** because it catches essentially all bacteria,
> and you need progressively **smaller** pores to catch **viruses.**

## The one thread through the whole lecture

Everything sorts into a 2×2 you can rebuild from memory:

|  | **Kills (-cide)** | **Inhibits (-static)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical** | Autoclave, incineration, dry-heat oven, ionizing radiation (sterilize); boiling, pasteurization, UV (disinfect) | Refrigeration, freezing, desiccation, osmotic pressure |
| **Chemical** | Oxidizing agents, aldehydes, gaseous sterilants (high-level) | Bacteriostatic low-level agents (quats, heavy metals) |
| **Mechanical** | — (removes, doesn't kill) | Filtration, HEPA, hand washing (degerming) |

And the single hardest thing to kill is the **bacterial endospore** — only **high-level /
sterilizing** methods (autoclave, incineration, dry-heat, ionizing radiation, ethylene oxide,
peracetic acid, long glutaraldehyde) reach it. Everything that only "disinfects" **leaves endospores
alive.**

| **-cide kills, -static stalls** | Suffix meanings (bactericide vs. bacteriostatic) |
| **Cleaner · Sanitizer · Disinfectant · Virucide · Sterilant** | Rising germicidal spectrum (only the sterilant gets spores) |
| **Physical · Chemical · Mechanical** | The three control categories |
| **121 / 15 / 15** | Autoclave: **121 °C**, **15 psi**, **~15 min** — kills all but prions |
| **Ionizing = deep & sterilizes; UV = shallow & disinfects** | The two radiations |
| **Cold · Dry · Salty = static, not cidal** | Refrigeration, desiccation, osmotic pressure only inhibit |
| **Spores survive disinfection** | Only high-level/sterilizing methods kill endospores |
| **Smaller pore, smaller catch** | Membrane filters (0.22 µm = the bacteria-sterilizing filter) |
