# Lecture Outline — Epidemiology

## 1. What epidemiology is 🎯
- The study of the **distribution and prevalence** of disease in a **population** — how much, where, in whom.
- Grew from "the study of **epidemics**" to the study of **everything affecting a population's health.**
- Cares about the **population, not the individual**; the goal is always **intervention.**
- **Nurses are on the forefront** — often the first to notice an epidemic starting.

## 2. Infection vs. disease vs. infectious disease
- **Infection** = an organism enters, grows, and causes symptoms (strep throat).
- **Disease** = altered tissue/metabolic function, **not** from an infectious agent (myasthenia gravis, anorexia nervosa).
- **Infectious disease** = microbe-caused tissue disruption, typically **chronic** (TB, AIDS).
- **Disease = morbidity** (the population-level word).

## 3. Classifying diseases — 5 ways
- By **causative agent** · by **body system** · by **longevity/severity** (chronic vs. acute) · by **how spread** · by **effect on populations.**

## 4. Population patterns 🎯
- **Sporadic** (scattered, unrelated) · **Epidemic** (short-term local rise) · **Endemic** (present all the time) · **Pandemic** (worldwide, 6 continents).
- Endemic examples: Ebola/W. Africa, Lyme/Texas, rabies/Austin bats, *Coccidioides*/SW US, diabetes in Hispanic & Black populations.
- Current pandemics: HIV/AIDS, TB, **COVID-19.**

## 5. Reproductive number (R₀) 🎯
- New cases generated by **one case** in a fully susceptible population — the measure of contagiousness.
- **COVID-19 R₀ ≫ influenza** → more contagious and more severe. *(Professor: "be sure you know reproductive number.")*

## 6. Morbidity & mortality
- **Morbidity** = number of **cases**; **mortality** = number of **deaths.** Given as **rates.**
- Worked example: 10 per 100,000 in a city of 1,000,000 → **100 cases.**
- First rule: don't agitate the public — speak in rates.

## 7. Prevalence vs. incidence 🎯
- **Prevalence** = total cases; **incidence** = **new** cases (how fast it's spreading).
- Prevalence 50 / incidence 2 → waning. Prevalence 50 / incidence 48 → big problem now.
- *(Professor: "I'm probably going to ask questions about that next Thursday.")*

## 8. Confounding data
- HPV **looked** like the #1 STI due to Gardasil-era over-testing; **chlamydia** is actually the most common.
- "If you go looking for infectious diseases, you'll find them."

## 9. Study of comparisons — Snow, patient zero, etiology
- Compare the sick to a **baseline** (1,000 flu cases in June = normal; 1 measles case = alarming).
- **John Snow / Broad Street pump** — father of epidemiology; mapped cholera, broke the pump handle = the **intervention.**
- Cluster cases → find **patient zero.**
- **Etiology** = study of causation; contact tracing; **Koch's postulates** → now molecular; ask **Place, People, Time.**

## 10. Data & surveillance
- Data is mostly **passive** (reported by facilities) and often **a year behind.**
- **Reportable diseases** → **CDC** (weekly Morbidity & Mortality Report) → **WHO.**
- **Folate / neural-tube-defect** win: fortify **masa**, not just bread, in South Texas.

## 11. Stages of infectious disease & outcomes
- **Incubation → prodromal → illness → decline → convalescence.**
- Contagious **before** symptoms; incubation length is key to intervening.
- Outcomes: **recover / death / impairment** (cognitive or physical, sometimes lifelong).

## 12. Modes of transmission + portals
- **Contact** (direct / indirect / droplet) · **Vehicle** (air / water / food) · **Vector** (biological / mechanical).
- **Portals of entry & exit** (often the same route); Guinea-worm portal-of-exit example.
- **Why outbreaks come in waves**: a mildly-ill person seeds each successive wave → distancing, self-quarantine, and masks break the chain.

## 13. Communicable vs. non-communicable · fomites
- **Communicable** = transmissible (incl. mother→fetus); **contagious** = highly communicable.
- **Non-communicable** = acquired but not passed on (Lyme disease).
- **Fomite** = inanimate object that transmits (doorknob, phone); vehicles = food/water/air, oral–fecal.
- Wet markets → protein exchange → **novel viruses**; bird flyways & airplanes spread agents globally.

## 14. Reservoirs, sources, carriers · vectors 🎯 · zoonosis 🎯
- **Reservoir** (where a pathogen lives) vs. **source** (where you got it) vs. **carrier** (a person who harbors & spreads).
- **#1 carrier group = children.**
- **Vector — biological vs. mechanical** 🎯 (mosquito vs. fly). ⛔ the vector chart (Slide 33) is not tested.
- **Zoonosis** 🎯 = animal infection transmissible to humans (≥150; animal→human only ⛔).
- **Nonliving reservoirs**: soil, water (Katrina floodwater).

## 15. Factors influencing epidemics — the host
- Overall health, **age extremes**, immunization, genetics, culture, religion.
- **Time of year: summer is worst** (people socialize); **July 4th** = #1 food-poisoning holiday.
- **Rural vs. city immunity** (hygiene idea): farm/ranch upbringing → stronger immunity from early microbial exposure.

## 16. Factors influencing epidemics — the pathogen
- Adherence, invasiveness, **mutation rate** (COVID 200+ strains/variants), **intracellular** survival, toxins.
- **Infectious dose**: Salmonella ≈ 100,000 vs. **Shigella ≈ 10** (more virulent). Incubation lets it spread unseen.

## 17. What weakens host defenses
- Age extremes, genetic/acquired (AIDS) immune defects, surgery/transplants (artificial portal → prophylactic antibiotics), cancer/liver/diabetes, chemo/immunosuppressants, secondary infections.
- **Fusarium** killed an immunosuppressed patient it wouldn't harm in a healthy host.

## 18. Pathogen survival strategies
- Cysts, endospores, mutation, intracellular life, complex life cycles, capsules (bacteria/yeast).

## 19. Controlling epidemics
- **Reduce the source** (quarantine, destroy infected hosts) · **disrupt transmission** (destroy vectors, pasteurization, water treatment) · **immunization** · hand washing · surveillance.
- You can be **arrested** for actively spreading **TB or AIDS** (not COVID).

## 20. Symptoms · signs · syndrome
- **Symptom** = subjective (patient-reported) · **Sign** = objective (measured) · **Syndrome** = the cluster used to diagnose.
- **Asymptomatic/subclinical** = no symptoms but possibly signs.

## 21. Nosocomial infections · universal precautions
- Hospital-acquired; **2–4 million/yr.** Types: **exogenous / endogenous / iatrogenic.**
- Control: **hand washing** (most effective), SOPs, **Universal Precautions / BSI** (treat all specimens as infectious).
- Mandatory **reporting** of rates drove numbers down.

## 22. Public health & reportable diseases
- Data at **city → state → national → global**; US Public Health Service + WHO; monitor water/food.
- **Reporting timelines:** immediately (**anthrax**), same day (**TB**), end of week (**COVID**), end of month (**STIs**).

## 23. The role of nurses 🎯
- **Primary defense against epidemics** — see the sick first (ER), spot patterns early, alert the team.
