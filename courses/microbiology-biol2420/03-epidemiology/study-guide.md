## 1. What epidemiology is 🎯

**Epidemiology** = the study of the **distribution** and **prevalence** of disease in a
**population** — how much disease there is, where, and in whom. *(Slides 5–6)*

- It started as just "the study of **epidemics**," but has grown to cover **everything that affects
  people**: hyperlipidemia, dog bites, motor-vehicle accidents — anything driving a population's
  mortality. *(Transcript)*
- Epidemiologists care about the **population as a whole, not the individual.** (The individual is
  looked after by the individual, then their physician and care team.) *(Slide 6; Transcript)*
- The whole point is **intervention**: every disease follows a **pattern**; if you can find the
  pattern, you can step in and stop the spread. *(Slide 8)*

> **Plain-language:** epidemiology is detective work on a whole city instead of one patient —
> find what the sick people have in common, then break that link.

> Slide 6 — the aims of epidemiology: describe the distribution and size of disease problems, and
> identify the causes so services can be planned and prevention prioritized.

## 2. Infection vs. disease vs. infectious disease

A **pathological state** means something is wrong with the body. Three terms the professor wants
kept straight *(Slides 2–3; Transcript)*:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Infection** | An organism gets into the body, grows on a constant nutrient supply, and produces symptoms | Strep throat |
| **Disease** | An alteration of the body's **tissue function** or **metabolic processes** — **not** caused by an infectious agent; can be mental or physical | Myasthenia gravis, anorexia nervosa |
| **Infectious disease** | The disruption of a tissue/organ caused by microbes or their products — typically **chronic**, affecting a person over a long period | Tuberculosis, AIDS |

- Strep throat *is* an infectious disease, but it's short (a few days of antibiotics). The ones
  epidemiology cares about most are the **long, chronic** ones (TB, AIDS). *(Transcript)*
- **Disease is also called *morbidity*.** *(Slide 3)*

> **Morbidity** = the pathological state seen across a **population** (number of cases). It's the
> population-level word for "how much sickness is here."

## 3. How diseases are classified — 5 ways

Epidemiologists and clinicians sort diseases so patterns are easier to spot. *(Slide 4; Transcript)*

1. **By causative agent** (taxonomic group) — e.g., someone has *coccidioidomycosis*, *tuberculosis*.
2. **By body system / part affected** — enter**itis**, encephal**itis**, mening**itis**, cellul**itis**.
3. **By longevity & severity** — **chronic** (long-lasting) vs. **acute** (very severe, very fast).
4. **By how they're spread** to the host — communicable, non-communicable, vector-borne, vehicle-borne, direct/indirect contact.
5. **By their effect on populations** (not individuals) — **sporadic, epidemic, endemic, pandemic.**

> Slide 4 — the same five buckets, straight from the deck. (Insurance uses the ICD-10 code book;
> epidemiologists use these five instead.)

## 4. Patterns in a population — sporadic · epidemic · endemic · pandemic 🎯

How a disease behaves **across a population** is the fifth classification, and the most useful.
*(Slides 8, 10–13; Transcript)*

| Pattern | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Sporadic** | A **few scattered cases**, in different places, **not related** — no real population effect | One or two idiopathic pertussis cases in different towns |
| **Epidemic** | A **short-term increase** in cases in a given population (short is relative — a week or six months) | A local whooping-cough or measles outbreak |
| **Endemic** | An agent present in a given population/location **all the time** | Coccidioides in the SW US; diabetes in Hispanic & Black populations |
| **Pandemic** | A **worldwide** increase — cases on **6 continents** (Antarctica doesn't count, too sparsely populated) | HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, **COVID-19** |

- **Endemic examples the professor gave:** Ebola in West Africa, Lyme disease in Texas, **rabies in
  Austin (bats)**, and *Coccidioides* endemic to S. Texas / S. New Mexico / S. Arizona / S. California
  (a dust-storm fungus). *(Slides 11, 13; Transcript)*
- **Pandemic right now:** besides HIV/AIDS and TB, **COVID-19** is the current pandemic. *(Transcript)*

> **Bats & rabies:** central Texas has three large bat colonies (Round Rock/620, under the Congress
> Ave. bridge downtown, and a big cave outside San Antonio). **Never touch a dead bat** — assume it
> died of rabies. Rabies dies with its host, but you can't tell how long the bat has been dead.
> *(Transcript — not test content, but a good endemic example.)*

> Slide 13 — the four occurrence maps side by side: sporadic (scattered dots), endemic (one region
> shaded), epidemic (regional outbreaks), pandemic (whole globe).

## 5. Reproductive number (R₀) 🎯

The **reproductive number (R₀)** = the **expected number of new cases generated by one case** in a
population where **everyone is susceptible**. It's how you measure how easily something spreads.
*(Slide 9; Transcript — professor: "be sure you know what reproductive number is.")*

- A **higher R₀ = more contagious.** On the flu-vs-COVID comparison table, **COVID-19 has a much
  higher R₀** than influenza — that's why it is both **more contagious and more severe.** *(Slide 9)*

> **Why it matters:** R₀ tells you whether one case tends to fizzle out (R₀ < 1) or explode into an
> outbreak (R₀ > 1). It's the single number epidemiologists reach for to describe spread.

> Slide 9 — the flu vs. COVID-19 table (R₀, incubation, hospitalization, case-fatality). You don't
> need the exact numbers; know that COVID's R₀ is the higher one.

---

## 6. Morbidity & mortality — given as rates

- **Morbidity** = number of **cases** (people who are sick). *(Slide 14)*
- **Mortality** = number of **deaths**. *(Slide 14)*
- Both are usually given as **rates** — e.g., "10 per 100,000." *(Transcript)*

**Worked example (know how to do this):** if COVID morbidity in Austin is **10 per 100,000** and
Austin's population is about **1,000,000**, then there are **10 segments** of 100,000, so
10 × 10 = **100 total cases.** *(Transcript)*

> **First rule of epidemiology:** don't put the public in an agitated state. Professionals speak in
> **rates/"code"** ("10 per 100,000") so the average person stays calm while the experts read the
> real signal. *(Transcript)*

---

## 7. Prevalence vs. incidence 🎯

The professor said he'd **ask about this on the exam.** *(Slides 16–17; Transcript)*

- **Prevalence** = the **total** number of cases in a given population/location (a whole number).
- **Incidence** = the number of **NEW** cases — this tells you **how fast** the disease is spreading.

**How to read them together:**
- Prevalence **50**, incidence **2** → only 2 new cases out of 50; the outbreak is **waning** (2 new,
  48 established). *Not worried.*
- Prevalence **50**, incidence **48** → 48 of the 50 are brand-new; you have a **big problem right
  now.**

> **Plain-language:** prevalence is how many people *currently have it*; incidence is how many
> *just caught it*. High incidence = the fire is still spreading.

> Slide 16 — prevalence = cases at any given time (people are getting better or newly infected);
> incidence = new cases, which tells us how fast it's spreading.

> **Read data by subgroup, not just in total** *(Transcript)*: plotting STIs by **age** shows the
> expected spike in the late-teens–20s **and a second spike in the elderly** — traced to more
> unprotected sex in nursing homes (partly linked to erectile-dysfunction drugs) and repeated
> reinfection. Same dataset, two very different stories once you break it down by group.

## 8. Confounding data

**Confounding** = when something distorts the data so a conclusion looks true but isn't. *(Transcript)*

- **The HPV example:** old data appeared to show **human papillomavirus** as the #1 STI. But it was
  confounded by the **Gardasil** debate era — clinicians were **hyper-vigilant and testing more**,
  so they *reported* more. *"If you go looking for infectious diseases, you're going to find them."*
- **The truth:** **chlamydia** is the most common/important STI for the average city (trichomoniasis
  is a big problem in some places).

> **Why this matters:** more testing can inflate a number without more actual disease. Always ask
> what the data-collection method was before trusting the ranking. *(Slide 15 is that STD table.)*

## 9. The study of comparisons — John Snow, patient zero & etiology

Epidemiology is fundamentally a **study of comparisons**: you compare the sick to a **baseline** to
know whether you have an outbreak. *(Slide 6; Transcript)*

- **Baseline matters more than raw numbers.** 1,000 flu cases in Austin in June is **normal** (summer
  flu season); **one measles case** is alarming — measles is rare and extremely contagious. The skill
  is **analyzing** what the data means, not memorizing numbers. *(Transcript)*

**John Snow & the Broad Street pump** *(Slides 8, 20; Transcript)* — the **father of epidemiology**:
- A cholera outbreak hit London. Snow put a **dot on a map** for every case and looked for what the
  sick had in common.
- The dots clustered around **one water pump** (Broad Street). He tested the water → **contaminated
  with cholera.**
- Officials wouldn't remove it, so (by legend) he **broke off the pump handle** — the **intervention**
  — and cholera stopped. This launched modern epidemiology.

**Finding the source — patient zero:** cluster the cases (dendrograms, point-inflection charts) to
find where infection started. That first person is the **initial case / patient zero.** *(Transcript)*

**Etiology = the study of causation.** Find the cause → intervene. *(Slide 19; Transcript)*
- **Contact tracing** does this in practice: with an STI like gonorrhea, epidemiologists ask each
  patient about partners, trace backward, and eventually reach **patient zero** to stop the spread.
- Some infections can be **cured** (gonorrhea), others only **managed** (AIDS).
- Historically this used **Koch's postulates** (animal models); today **molecular techniques** do it
  in **hours/days** instead of weeks. *(Slide 19)*
- What epidemiologists always ask — **Place · People · Time**: where were you, who were you with,
  and when/how long. *(Slide 20)*

> Slide 20 — the Broad Street cholera map: every dot is a case, clustered on the pump.

> **Koch's postulates have limits** *(Slide 19)*: some pathogens can't be cultured, some diseases
> need multiple pathogens/cofactors, and ethics forbid infecting a human host — so the postulates
> can't always be satisfied.

## 10. Data & surveillance — mostly passive, often a year behind

- Most epidemiologic data is **passive**: epidemiologists rely on **healthcare facilities to report**
  it. The system is only as good as the reporters. *(Slide 7; Transcript)*
- Real-time tracking is too expensive, so analysis is often about **a year behind** — you study last
  year's data to find this year's trends. *(Transcript)*
- **Reportable diseases** must, by law, be reported to authorities → the **CDC** (Atlanta), whose
  weekly **Morbidity and Mortality Report** shares statistics with the **WHO**. *(Slide 7)*

> **The folate / neural-tube-defect win** *(Transcript)*: South Texas (McAllen area) had rising
> **spina bifida** (a neural-tube defect). Folate — needed to build the **myelin** around the spinal
> cord — was fortified in **bread**, but the population ate **tortillas**, not bread. The fix: add
> folate to **masa (corn)** so everyone gets it. A clean example of data → intervention. *(The
> professor worked on this study for his epidemiology master's.)*

## 11. Stages of infectious disease & possible outcomes

Following infection, disease moves through a predictable sequence — the **disease process.**
*(Slides 21–22; Transcript)*

**The five classic stages** *(Slide 21)*:
1. **Incubation period** — from exposure until signs/symptoms appear (you can be **contagious before
   you feel sick**).
2. **Prodromal period** — earliest, vague symptoms begin.
3. **Illness** — full signs and symptoms; usually most infectious here.
4. **Decline** — symptoms ease as the body wins.
5. **Convalescence** — recovery (you may **still be infectious**).

**Key incubation point** *(Transcript)*: if your incubation was 2 weeks before diagnosis, you may
have exposed many people **before you even knew** — that's why knowing the incubation period is
essential to intervene.

**Possible outcomes** — there's a whole outcomes science under epidemiology *(Slide 22; Transcript)*:
- **Recover** (convalesce)
- **Death**
- **Impairment** — **cognitive or physical**, sometimes **for life** (e.g., meningococcal
  meningitis, encephalitis). The infectious agent can be **long gone** and still leave lasting harm.

> Slide 22 — exposure → preclinical symptoms → clinical symptoms → the intervention window (quarantine,
> medicate, vaccinate) → outcome (recover / death / impairment).

## 12. Modes of transmission + portals of entry & exit

**Three groups of transmission** *(Slide 23; Transcript)*:

| Group | Sub-types | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Contact** | Direct · indirect · droplet | Direct = sex; indirect = shaking hands / a contaminated object; droplet = a sneeze |
| **Vehicle** | Airborne · waterborne · foodborne | Contaminated water; food poisoning |
| **Vector** | Biological · mechanical | Mosquito (biological); housefly (mechanical) — see §14 |

**Portals of entry and exit** *(Slides 24; Transcript)*: pathogens leave through a **portal of exit**
(often the **same** route as entry) — usually in the body's **secretions/excretions.** Knowing both
lets you teach people how to break the chain.

> **The Guinea worm — a portal-of-exit lesson** *(Transcript)*: larvae penetrate the skin while
> people wade in water; the worm grows under the skin, and the female **pops out to lay eggs when
> water touches the skin.** The Gates Foundation taught people to slowly **roll the emerging worm
> onto a twig** — closing the portal of exit and nearly eliminating the disease.

> Slide 24 — a body diagram of the portals of exit: eyes (tears), nose/mouth (secretions), skin,
> anus (feces), urethra/genitals, ears, etc.

**Why outbreaks come in waves** *(Transcript)*: a **mildly-symptomatic** person keeps their routine
(the store, the gas station, three classes of students) and unknowingly exposes dozens — and each of
*those* people seeds the **next** wave. Cases surge in repeating **inflection points.** This is
exactly why **social distancing, self-quarantine, and masks** work: they cut the exposures that would
start the next wave. *(A worker with no sick leave who comes in sick is a big driver.)*

## 13. Communicable vs. non-communicable · fomites

- **Communicable disease** = an infected host **can transmit** the agent to another host (contagious).
  Includes **vertical** transfer (mother → developing fetus). *(Slide 25)*
- **Contagious** = **highly** communicable.
- **Non-communicable disease** = you can **acquire** it but **won't pass it** to another person by
  contact. *(Slide 25)*
  - Example: **Lyme disease** — you catch it from a vector (tick), but you can't give it to someone
    by touching them.
  - Also non-communicable: a **compromised person invaded by their own microbiota**, or **accidental
    contact with a microbe in a nonliving reservoir**.

**Indirect transmission & fomites** *(Slide 26; Transcript)*:
- **Fomite** = any **inanimate object** that can transmit an infectious agent — **doorknobs, phones,
  contaminated objects.**
- **Vehicles** for indirect spread: **food, water, air** (indoor air, **droplet nuclei, aerosols**),
  and the **oral–fecal** route.

> **Wet markets & novel viruses** *(Transcript)*: the close proximity of many animals and people
> allows an **exchange of proteins** that drives **mutations** → **novel viruses** (novel coronavirus,
> novel avian influenza). Fully cooking the meat removes the risk; the danger is the mixing, not the
> culture itself.

> **How agents cross the globe** *(Transcript)*: overlapping **bird-migration flyways** can carry an
> agent from Asia to North America — or, faster, a **sick person on an airplane** (a doctor carried
> **Ebola** from Liberia to New Jersey).

## 14. Reservoirs, sources & carriers · vectors 🎯 · zoonosis

**Reservoir** = the primary habitat in nature from which a pathogen originates — any **living thing
or inanimate object** that houses an agent and can spread it. **Source** = the specific individual/
object from which an infection is **actually acquired.** *(Slide 31; Transcript)*

- Reservoirs are **mostly animals**, but water, soil, and phones count too. Terminology varies —
  don't get thrown when people use "reservoir" loosely. *(Transcript)*
- **Carrier** = a **person** who harbors an agent and can spread it, often with **little/no symptoms.**
  Types: asymptomatic, incubation, convalescent, chronic, passive. *(Slide 31)*
- **The #1 carrier group = children** ("kiddos") — strep throat, hand-foot-and-mouth, etc. Kids get
  dirty and build immunity, but pass a lot around. *(Transcript)*

**Vectors — know biological vs. mechanical** 🎯 *(Slide 32; Transcript — a flagged exam point)*:

| Vector type | How it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Biological vector** | **Actively participates** in the pathogen's life cycle; takes a **blood meal**, then transmits | Mosquito → malaria |
| **Mechanical vector** | Just **carries** the agent on its body — **not infected itself** | Housefly lands on feces, then on your sandwich |

> Slide 33 — the vector-borne infections table. ⛔ *"I don't need you to know this chart."* Just know
> **what a vector is** and the **biological-vs-mechanical** difference.

**Zoonosis** 🎯 = an infection **indigenous to animals but naturally transmissible to humans.** *(Slide 34;
Transcript)*
- Humans **don't** contribute to the microbe's persistence; can involve **multiple hosts**; **at
  least 150** worldwide. *(Slide 34)*
- Spelling note: **zoonosis** (one) vs. **zoonoses** (more than one) — not a typo. *(Transcript)*
- ⛔ This course only cares about **animal → human**, not animal-to-animal. *(Transcript)*

**Nonliving reservoirs** *(Slide 35; Transcript)*: **soil** and **water.** Example: post-**Katrina**
floodwater became a contaminated cesspool (backed-up sewage/septic) — a nonliving reservoir for
disease.

## 15. Factors that influence epidemics — the host

**Host susceptibility** — how likely a person is to come down with disease *(Slides 36–37; Transcript)*:

- **Overall health** and **age** — the **very old** (lose the ability to fight infection) and the
  **very young** (adaptive immunity not yet developed → rely on **cell-mediated** immunity) are most
  susceptible.
- **Immunization** status.
- **Genetic background** — some people are more predisposed.
- **Culture** — e.g., "big hugger" cultures spread respiratory infections more easily.
- **Religion** — some beliefs decline vaccines/medical care, changing survival odds.
- **Time of year** 🎯 — counter-intuitively, **summer is the worst**, because people are **out
  socializing.** The **4th of July** is the #1 holiday for **food poisoning**, followed by
  **Thanksgiving.** *(Slide 37; Transcript)*

> Slide 36 — the host-susceptibility factors: immunization, genetics, health, age extremes, culture,
> religion.

> **Rural vs. city immunity (the hygiene idea)** *(Transcript)*: people raised on a **farm/ranch**
> tend to have **stronger immune systems** than lifelong city dwellers, because **early exposure to
> many microbes trains immunity.** It's also why letting kids get a little dirty helps them build
> defenses — a concern now that many spend their days indoors on screens.

## 16. Factors that influence epidemics — the pathogen

The pathogen's own traits also drive an epidemic *(Slides 38–41; Transcript)*:

- **Adherence** — its ability to **attach and stay** on the host.
- **Invasiveness** — how easily it gets into the body.
- **Antigenic variation (mutation rate)** — fast mutators are hard to treat; you fix one form and it
  changes. COVID-19 has **200+ strains** (the UK, South African, and Brazil **variants** were the
  dangerous ones). *(Slide 39; Transcript)*
- **Intracellular survival** — can it live **inside** cells and evade immunity? (HIV/AIDS, malaria,
  chlamydia, rickettsia.) *(Slide 39)*
- **Toxin production** — e.g., botulism. *(Slide 40)*
- **Infectious dose** — how many organisms are needed to cause infection: **Salmonella ≈ 100,000
  cells**, but **Shigella only ≈ 10 cells** (far more virulent). *(Slide 40)*
- **Incubation period** — you can transmit **before you know you're infected** (HIV, chlamydia).
  *(Slide 41)*

> **Plain-language:** a lower **infectious dose** means a nastier bug — it takes only a handful of
> Shigella to make you sick, versus a hundred thousand Salmonella.

## 17. What weakens host defenses

Conditions that **compromise defense barriers or immune responses** *(Slide 42 / Table 13.4;
Transcript)*:

- **Old age and extreme youth**
- **Genetic defects** in immunity, and **acquired defects** (e.g., **AIDS**)
- **Surgery and organ transplants** — create an **artificial portal of entry** (why **prophylactic
  antibiotics** are given after surgery)
- **Organic disease** — cancer, liver malfunction, **diabetes**
- **Chemotherapy / immunosuppressive drugs** — and the **stress** of illness
- **Secondary (other) infections** — e.g., someone hospitalized for flu/COVID who then dies of a
  **bacterial pneumonia** picked up while there

> **Fusarium story** *(Transcript)*: a cancer patient in remission but still **immunosuppressed**
> mowed the lawn, inhaled dust carrying the fungus **Fusarium**, and died within days. The same
> fungus does nothing to a person with a **competent** immune system — susceptibility is everything.

## 18. Pathogen survival strategies

Ways pathogens persist and dodge the immune system *(Slide 43; Transcript)*:

- **Cyst formation** — dormant stage (protozoans)
- **Endospores**
- **Mutation** (antigenic variation) — RNA/enveloped viruses mutate fast
- **Intracellular** lifestyle
- **Complex life cycles** — protozoans and metazoan parasites
- **Capsules** — made by bacteria and yeasts

## 19. Controlling epidemics

Levers epidemiologists pull to stop spread *(Slides 44–46; Transcript)*:

- **Reduce the source** — **quarantine** (animals or people; quarantining people works poorly), or
  **destroy the infected host** (whole herds have been culled; contaminated meat discarded). *(Slide 44)*
- **Disrupt the chain of transmission** — **destroy vectors**, **pasteurization** (60 °C / 30 min;
  flash 72 °C / 15 s), **water treatment.** *(Slide 45)*
- **Immunization** — the **best way to protect a whole population** is to have as many people immunized
  as possible. *(Slide 46)*
- **Hand washing** — critical, especially against hospital-acquired infections. *(Transcript)*
- **Surveillance** — rely on medical institutions to report; remember we're ~a year behind. *(Transcript)*

> **Legal note** *(Transcript)*: there are **two diseases you can be arrested for actively spreading
> — tuberculosis and AIDS.** There is **no such law for COVID-19.**

## 20. Symptoms · signs · syndrome

How clinicians describe illness *(Slides 50–51; Transcript)*:

- **Symptom** = **subjective** — felt/reported by the **patient** ("I feel clammy / cold").
- **Sign** = **objective** — **measured by a practitioner** (blood pressure, temperature, orthostatics).
- **Syndrome** = a **group of signs + symptoms** that together characterize a disease → used to
  **diagnose.**
- **Asymptomatic / subclinical** infections lack symptoms but may still show **signs.** *(Slide 52)*

---

## 21. Nosocomial (hospital-acquired) infections · universal precautions

**Nosocomial infection** = an infection **acquired or developing during a hospital stay** — about
**2–4 million** cases a year in the US. *(Slides 47, 53; Transcript)*

**Three types** *(Slide 54; Transcript)*:

| Type | Source |
|---|---|
| **Exogenous** | Pathogen acquired **from the healthcare environment** |
| **Endogenous** | Arises from your **own normal microbiota** (turned infectious in the hospital setting) |
| **Iatrogenic** | Results from a **medical procedure** |

**Control** *(Slides 55–56; Transcript)*:
- **Hand washing** is the **single most effective** way to reduce nosocomial infections.
- **Standard operating procedures** and **precautions.**
- **Universal Precautions (UPs)** — CDC guidelines: **assume every patient specimen could be
  infectious**; use **body-substance isolation (BSI).** Treat everything from a human as potentially
  infectious and you minimize your risk. *(Slide 56)*

> **Reporting drove numbers down** *(Transcript)*: a law (~8 years before the lecture) required every
> hospital to **report its nosocomial-infection rate.** Once staff saw their own numbers, rates fell —
> tracking creates accountability.

## 22. Public health & reportable diseases

Data is analyzed at **city → state → national → global** levels; the **US Public Health Service** is
the national agency and the **WHO** coordinates internationally. They monitor **water and food
safety** and run **public-education** campaigns. *(Slide 57; Transcript)*

**Reporting timelines** — some diseases must be reported faster than others *(Slide 48; Transcript)*:

| Report… | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Immediately** | **Anthrax** (also rabies, botulism, salmonella, shigella per the deck) |
| **Same day** | **Tuberculosis** |
| **End of the week** | **COVID-19** case counts |
| **End of the month** | **Sexually transmitted infections** |

> Slide 58 — the **Nationally Notifiable Infectious Diseases** list (Table 13.13) — the full set of
> reportable conditions. You don't memorize the list; know that **reporting timelines vary by
> disease.**

## 23. The role of nurses 🎯 (the theme of the whole deck)

**Nurses (and healthcare workers) are the primary defense against epidemics.** *(Slides 1, 49;
Transcript)*

- They are **on the front line** and **see things before anyone else** — really sick people go to
  the **ER**, not their family doctor.
- A nurse who notices **the same symptoms in more and more patients** is often the **first to spot an
  epidemic beginning** — which is exactly the surveillance signal epidemiology depends on.
- The professor's charge: talk to each other and to physicians when patterns appear; this vocation is
  about **helping people.**

> Slide 49 — "NURSES!! They see things before anyone else." The reason the deck is titled "the role
> of nurses in the identification of an epidemic."

## Quick recap — the terms most likely to be tested

| Term | One-liner |
|---|---|
| **Reproductive number (R₀)** 🎯 | New cases one case creates in a fully susceptible population — higher = more contagious |
| **Prevalence vs. incidence** 🎯 | Total cases vs. NEW cases (incidence = how fast it's spreading) |
| **Sporadic / epidemic / endemic / pandemic** | Scattered / short-term local rise / always present / worldwide |
| **Morbidity vs. mortality** | Cases vs. deaths (given as rates, e.g., per 100,000) |
| **Biological vs. mechanical vector** 🎯 | Part of the life cycle (mosquito) vs. just a carrier (fly) |
| **Zoonosis** 🎯 | Animal infection naturally transmissible to humans |
| **Communicable vs. non-communicable** | Can pass person-to-person vs. cannot (e.g., Lyme) |
| **Fomite** | Inanimate object that transmits (doorknob, phone) |
| **Reservoir / source / carrier** | Where it lives / where you got it / a person who harbors & spreads it |
| **Exogenous / endogenous / iatrogenic** | Nosocomial infection from environment / own microbiota / a procedure |
| **Symptom / sign / syndrome** | Patient-felt / measured / the cluster that names the disease |
| **Infectious dose** | Organisms needed to infect (Shigella 10 ≪ Salmonella 100,000) |
