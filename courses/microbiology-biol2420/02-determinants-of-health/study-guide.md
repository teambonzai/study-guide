## 1. What determines good health 🎯

The professor opens with three overlapping pieces of "health." *(Slide 2)*

- **Physical health** — measurable: blood chemistry, blood pressure, BMI *(he notes BMI is "not a
  really good indicator" but people use it)*.
- **Mental health** — *"if you believe you're not healthy, you are more susceptible to getting sick."*
  ~20% of the U.S. population has some mental-health challenge.
- **Spiritual health** — **not** the same as being religious. It's *having a purpose* — a reason to get
  up in the morning. People who are spiritual/optimistic tend to be more upbeat and, he argues, recover
  better: a hopeful patient's body *"produces certain molecules that help fight off disease."*

> **Why it matters in a micro class:** getting an infection isn't only about the microbe. It's about
> *why you were exposed* — the cultural, social, and health-status context around the exposure.

## 2. Determinants & factors that affect health 🎯

Whole clusters of factors drive disease and infection. *(Slides 3–5)*

- **Social determinants of health** *(Slide 3)* — income & social status, education, employment/working
  conditions, social support, culture, gender, physical & social environments, health services, healthy
  child development, biology & genetics.
- **Factors that affect health — the pyramid** *(Slide 4)*: interventions ranked by impact.
  **Socioeconomic factors** have the **largest** impact (base); then changing the context to make the
  healthy choice the default; then long-lasting protective interventions; then clinical interventions;
  then **counseling & education** (smallest impact, tip).
- **Complex disease wheel** *(Slide 5)* — genetics, environment, host factors, social factors, and
  **epigenetics** all interact to shape disease.

**The single biggest driver, in his view: the socioeconomic factor — and the lever is education.**
Not necessarily college; it's helping people understand *why* to follow a treatment plan and take their
meds. *(Transcript)*

> **In class example:** most people who caught COVID were those who **couldn't work from home** —
> service-industry workers whose skill set forced in-person exposure. Hence his push for *any* real
> skill set (plumber, carpenter, mechanic), not just a degree. *(Transcript)*

> **A determinant in action:** the **highest incidence of new HIV/AIDS cases** is now in **young
> Latina/Hispanic women** — a social-determinant pattern the professor wants to address through
> **education** about limiting exposure. *(Transcript)*

## 3. Pathogenic, opportunistic, and virulent 🎯

Three definitions the professor wants cold. *(Slides 6–7)*

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Pathogenic** | Disease-**causing**; an organism's ability to cause disease | *E. coli* O157:H7 |
| **Opportunistic pathogen** | Normally **harmless** in its usual habitat, but becomes pathogenic when the host is **debilitated / immunocompromised**, or when it lands in the **wrong site** | *Pneumocystis jirovecii* → pneumonia in AIDS patients; *E. coli* from the gut → UTI |
| **Virulent** | A **high degree** of ability to cause infection | Ebola (~80% mortality) |

**Worked question — "which is more virulent?"** *E. coli* O157:H7 (the burger pathogen) **or** *E. coli*
in the gut? **Both are pathogenic** (both *can* cause disease), but **O157:H7 is more virulent** — in
~20% of people it causes **hemolytic uremic syndrome** (in the blood, attacks the kidneys, ~20%
mortality). Gut *E. coli* is an **opportunistic** pathogen: it only causes trouble in the wrong
conditions (e.g., a sterile site like the urinary tract). *(Slide 7; Transcript)*

> **Plain-language:** *pathogenic* = "can it cause disease?" *Virulent* = "how hard does it hit?"
> *Opportunistic* = "only when it gets the chance."

## 4. Portals of entry 🎯

**Portal of entry** = the site where a microorganism **enters** the body — cuts, lesions, injection
sites, or natural body orifices. **Four major pathways** *(Slide 8)*:

1. **Skin** — through a **break/breach** (cut, scratch, rash). Intact skin blocks almost everything.
2. **Mucous membranes** — the moist linings open to the environment (respiratory tract is the most
   common; also GI tract for acid-surviving pathogens).
3. **Placenta** — from mother to developing fetus.
4. **Parenteral route** — bypassing the skin/mucosa, e.g., **IV drug use / shared needles**.

> **In class:** the opioid crisis fuels a second crisis — **HIV and hepatitis B/C spread by shared
> needles** (the parenteral route). Volunteer clean-needle programs try to break that chain. *(Transcript)*

---

## 5. Contamination vs. infection 🎯

*(Slide 9)*

- **Contamination** = microbes are merely **present** on a surface or in a liquid (your skin, a cutting
  board, a water bottle). An **inanimate object can be contaminated, not infected.**
- **Infection** = the organism has **evaded the body's external defenses, multiplied, and become
  established** inside the body — and is now *doing something* to tissues.

> **Example:** your skin can be *contaminated* with *E. coli* and you're fine. If that *E. coli* gets
> into the bloodstream through a break in the skin and causes **bacteremia**, now it's an infection.
> A cutting board with *Salmonella* is **contaminated** (or a **reservoir**) — never "infected."

---

## 6. Disease vs. infection 🎯

A **disease** is a **harmful alteration** of host tissues or metabolic processes — it does **not** require
a microbe. An **infection** always involves a microbe growing in the body. *(Slides 13–14)*

- **Diseases (no microbe needed):** cystic fibrosis, cancer, diabetes, cardiovascular heart disease
  (CHD), depression, alcoholism *(yes — alcoholism is a disease)*, autoimmune disease (lupus). Tissue
  example: **arthritis**; metabolic example: **diabetes/hyperglycemia**.
- **Infections** are named with **-itis** (inflammation from a microbe): cellulitis (pyoderma),
  pharyngitis, conjunctivitis, pneumonia, cystitis, otitis media, vaginitis, colitis, gastritis,
  encephalitis. *(Slide 13)*

> **The -itis clue:** an "-itis" ending means **inflammation** — usually a microbe interacting with the
> tissue. *(Transcript)*

## 7. Classifying disease — the etiology terms 🎯

**Etiology** = the study of the **cause** of a disease. **Germ theory** says disease can be caused by
infection with pathogenic microbes; **Robert Koch** wrote the **postulates** you must satisfy to prove a
specific microbe causes a specific disease. *(Slide 15)*

Diseases are classified by cause. The professor says: **"Starting with immunological and going down, I
need you to know those terms."** *(Slides 16–17; Transcript)*

| 🎯 Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Immunological** | Immune-system dysfunction — **hypo**active (fails) or **hyper**active (overreacts → anaphylaxis) |
| **Neoplastic** | **Tumor / cancer** *(many cancers are triggered by infectious agents)* |
| **Iatrogenic** | Caused by a **medical procedure / treatment** (an operation, a device) |
| **Idiopathic** | Cause is **unknown** — you have the problem but don't know how it got there |
| **Nosocomial** | **Hospital-acquired** — broaden it to **all healthcare settings** (daycare, assisted living, hospice) |

**Modern replacements for "nosocomial"** *(know these)*:
- **HAI** = **Healthcare-Associated Infection**
- **CAI** = **Community-Acquired Infection** *(e.g., the Austin swim/wrestling teams that got*
  ***Staph aureus*** *skin lesions)*

> **⛔ Not tested:** the other classification terms — **heredity, congenital, degenerative, nutritional,
> endocrine, mental** — *"important, but outside the scope of this course."*

## 8. The infectious process 🎯

**Infection** = the **entry, manifestation, growth, and proliferation** of an infectious agent within a
host. *(Slide 18)*

For an infection to take hold, **three things must happen** *(Slides 18, 23; repeated in class)*:

1. The microorganism must **enter host tissues**.
2. It must **establish itself and begin to grow (propagate)**.
3. It must have **access to nutrients**.

**If any one of the three is blocked, the infection won't manifest.** *(Slide 23)*

> **Ties back to control of microbes:** antibiotics work by **halting growth** — knock out step 2 or 3
> (growth or nutrients) and you halt the infectious process. **Example:** HIV needs its nutrient source
> — the **T4 (CD4) helper** cell — in order to propagate. *(Transcript)*

---

## 9. Infection vs. intoxication (food poisoning vs. foodborne infection) 🎯

Both can come from microbes, but they are different processes. *(Slides 24–25)*

- **Intoxication** = illness from a **toxin** the microbe made — often the microbe isn't even growing in
  you (it grew in the food and left the toxin behind). **Short, sharp, no fever.**
- **Infection** = the **organism itself grows** in the body. **Longer course, with fever.**

| | **Intoxication — *S. aureus*** (food poisoning) | **Infection — *Salmonella*** (foodborne illness) |
|---|---|---|
| Onset | **~4 hours** | **3–14 days** |
| Duration | ~**24 hours** (until you vomit out the toxin) | **until treated** |
| Symptoms | **Vomiting** | **Cramps, fever, diarrhea, dehydration, tenesmus** |
| Fever? | **No** | **Yes** |

**Fever is the giveaway of a (bacterial) infection.** Intoxications **never** cause fever. *(Transcript)*
**Diagnosis of the Salmonella case:** a **stool sample** to find the agent, then **antibiotics**. *(Slide 25)*

> **In class — *Staph* enterotoxin:** **Enterotoxin F** from *Staphylococcus aureus* causes **toxic shock
> syndrome**. *(Slide 24)*
> **Carrier example:** **Typhoid Mary** (Mary Mallon) was a healthy **carrier** of *Salmonella* Typhi —
> a classic epidemiology story. *(Transcript)*
> **Tenesmus** = the urge to defecate, but nothing passes. *(Transcript)*

Some microbes are **risk factors** for cancer. *(Slide 20; Slides 19, 21–22 image diagrams)*

| **HPV** (human papillomavirus) | **Cervical** cancer *(and anal / oral cancers)* |

## 11. Virulence factors — the 4 categories 🎯

A **virulence factor** is a molecule a pathogen expresses/secretes that gives it a competitive edge in the
host — helping it **colonize** (adhere), **evade the immune response**, **enter/exit** cells, and **obtain
nutrients**. *(Slides 26–29)*

- **Pathogenicity** = the **ability** to cause disease.
- **Virulence** = the **degree** of pathogenicity.
- Some factors are **intrinsic** to the bacterium (capsule, endotoxin); others are picked up from
  **plasmids** (some toxins). *(Slide 29)*

**The professor "makes it easy" — know virulence factors in 4 categories** *("this is multiple choice"):*
*(Slide 28; Transcript)*

1. **Structural** — part of the cell architecture: **mycolic acid, peptidoglycan, capsule, viral
   envelope, endospore, flagella, fimbriae.** *(He won't test the details — "you're well versed" — just know they're category #1.)*
2. **Toxins** — mostly **bacterial** toxins (see §12).
3. **Enzymes** — extracellular enzymes (see §13).
4. **Anti-phagocytic factors** — proteins that block phagocytosis (see §14).

> **Memory hook: "S-T-E-A"** — **S**tructural, **T**oxins, **E**nzymes, **A**nti-phagocytic. *(Added — not
> the professor's; his four categories are exact.)*

**Reconciling the slide list:** the deck *(Slide 28)* actually lists **five** contributing factors —
**adhesion factors, biofilms, extracellular enzymes, toxins, antiphagocytic factors.** The professor
collapses these into his spoken **four** categories: **adhesion factors and biofilms are "structural"**
(they're cell-surface/architecture features), and the rest map directly. So the slide's five and his four
say the same thing.

**Adhesion** *(Slide 31)* is the very first step of establishing an infection: the **process by which
microorganisms attach to host cells** — required to establish colonies. It uses **adhesion factors** —
**specialized structures** (e.g., **fimbriae**) and **attachment proteins**. *(Slide 27 opens the whole
virulence-factor idea with "colonization of a niche — this includes adhesion to cells.")*

**Biofilm** — a community of microbes stuck to a surface in a protective matrix; a structural virulence
strategy that helps a pathogen persist and resist defenses.

## 12. Toxins — exotoxins vs. endotoxins 🎯

*"They will be on the exam — be sure you know them."* **Toxemia** = toxins carried in the bloodstream
beyond the infection site. *(Slides 32–35; Slide 36 comparison image)*

| | **Exotoxins** | **Endotoxins** |
|---|---|---|
| Source | **Actively secreted** by living bacteria (mostly Gram-positive **and** Gram-negative) | **Part of the cell wall** — the **lipid A** of the **LPS** in **Gram-negative** bacteria |
| Chemistry | **Protein / short peptide** | **Lipopolysaccharide (lipid/sugar)** |
| Toxicity | **Very high** — a tiny amount is enough | **Low** — needs **high numbers/volume** to harm |
| Heat | **Heat-labile** — can be **denatured** | **Heat-stable** — can't be broken apart |
| Vaccine? | **Yes** — denature it into a **toxoid** that keeps its **epitopes** → make an **antitoxin (IgG)** | **No** — weak antigenic specificity; **not feasible** to vaccinate against |
| Fever? | **No** *(botulism → no fever)* | **Yes** — it's a **pyrogen** |
| Modes of action | **Cytotoxin** (kills cells), **neurotoxin** (nerves), **enterotoxin** (vomiting) | Fever, dehydration/hypovolemia, **blood coagulation**, **septic (endotoxic) shock** → death in high numbers |
| Example organisms | Botulism, tetanus, gas gangrene, diphtheria, cholera, plague, staph food poisoning | Typhoid, tularemia, UTIs, meningococcal meningitis; **endotoxic shock** |

**Key exotoxin facts:** the two most potent exotoxins known are **tetanus toxin (tetanospasmin)** from
*Clostridium tetani* and **botulinum toxin** from *Clostridium botulinum* — **1 gram of botulinum toxin
could kill hundreds of thousands.** Other exotoxin producers include *Escherichia coli*, *Vibrio cholerae*
(**cholera**), *Clostridium perfringens* (**food poisoning / gas gangrene**), and *Clostridium difficile*
(**pseudomembranous colitis**). We make **antitoxins** (IgG) against denatured toxin — the tetanus and
botulism vaccines target the **toxin**, not the organism. Same idea as **antivenom** for snakebite.
*(Slides 33–34; Transcript)*

**Key endotoxin fact — clinical caution:** every Gram-negative organism **is** endotoxic by definition.
If you hit a big Gram-negative load with antibiotics and **burst all the cells at once**, you release
**more free lipid A** (more surface area) → the patient can get **worse**. Treat carefully. *(Slide 33; Transcript)*

> 📷 **Slide 36** — the "Comparison of Toxins" chalkboard (**exotoxins** = secreted, protein, high
> toxicity, toxoidable; **endotoxins** = part of the architecture, lipid/sugar, variable toxicity,
> **non**-toxoidable). Learn this side-by-side.

## 13. Enzymes as virulence factors 🎯

**Extracellular enzymes** are secreted by the pathogen to **dissolve structural chemicals** in the body —
helping it maintain the infection, invade further, and dodge defenses. *(Slides 37–38)*

| Enzyme | What it does | Made by |
|---|---|---|
| **Lipase** | Breaks down **oleic acid** and other protective skin fatty acids → **pimples/blemishes** | *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus* |
| **Coagulase** | **Coagulates plasma** — forms a fibrin wall around a clot; bacteria grow inside → **abscess / boil** | *Staphylococcus aureus* |
| **Hyaluronidase** | The **"spread factor"** — destroys **hyaluronic acid**, the "cement" holding cells together → bacteria spread fast through tissue | *Streptococcus pyogenes*, *S. aureus* (the "flesh-eaters") |

> **Skin-care asides *(from lecture)*:** don't over-use **astringents** or **loofahs** — they strip the
> body's natural chemical barriers and harbor bacteria. Men: use a **sharp razor** (a dull one nicks the
> skin → entry points). *(Transcript)*

## 14. Anti-phagocytic factors 🎯

Proteins that **stop phagocytes from engulfing** the microbe. *(No slide — taught out loud; "that's all
you need to know.")* *(Transcript)*

- **Protein A** — made by **_Staphylococcus aureus_**.
- **M protein** — made by **_Streptococcus pyogenes_**.
- **Capsule** — you can argue the capsule is anti-phagocytic too (and it is), but the two he wants are
  **protein A** and **M protein**.

> **Why lowercase "m"?** He notes it's written **M protein** (capital M) vs. **protein A** — different
> naming just because different research groups named them their own way. *(Transcript)*

## 15. The body's defenses — the three lines 🎯

The rest of the lecture pivots from *how microbes attack* to *how we defend*. *(Slides 39–58)*

- **Innate (non-specific)** — present from birth, acts on **all** invaders:
  - **1st line** — physical & chemical **barriers** (skin, mucous membranes, flushing, chemicals).
  - **2nd line** — **interferon, phagocytosis, complement**.
- **Adaptive / acquired (specific)** — the **3rd line** — **immunity via immunoglobulins**; **acquired
  over your lifetime**; run by **B and T lymphocytes** *(next lecture)*.

**Innate immunity** = **nonspecific** defense that acts **immediately or within hours** of an antigen
appearing — physical barriers, blood chemicals, and cells that attack foreign material. *(Slides 41–42)*

> you should be familiar with it."*

## 16. First line — physical & chemical barriers 🎯

**Physical barriers** *(Slide 43)*:
- **Skin** — the largest organ; while intact, it stops **almost everything**. Infections can be
  superficial, mid-layer, or full-thickness.
- **Mucous membranes** — line the openings to the body (ears, mouth, nose, anus, urethra).

**Chemical defenses** *(Slide 47)*:
- **Sebaceous secretions** — sebum & **oleic acid** (antimicrobial)
- **Lysozyme** — in **tears and saliva**; breaks down bacterial cell walls
- **Lactic acid & electrolytes** in **sweat** (hypertonic — few microbes survive)
- **Skin's acidic pH** and fatty-acid content
- **HCl** in the stomach
- **Digestive juices & bile** in the intestine
- **Semen** — carries antimicrobial chemicals
- **Acidic pH of the vagina** — *don't alter the vaginal environment unless medically necessary, or you
  invite infection.*

## 17. Flushing mechanisms & the mucociliary escalator 🎯

**Flushing mechanisms** physically wash microbes out — easy to overlook as defenses, but they count.
*(Slide 45)*

- **Sneezing · coughing · lacrimation (tears) · sweating · ear wax (cerumen) · salivation · defecation ·
  urination · peristalsis** (movement of a food bolus through the GI tract).

**Mucociliary escalator** *(Slide 46)* — **ciliated cells** in the trachea and lungs sweep trapped debris
and mucus **up** to the back of the throat, where you cough it out. Toward the end of a respiratory
infection, mucus thickens because it's loaded with **debris and white blood cells**.

> 📷 **Slide 46** — the respiratory tract + cilia driving the mucociliary escalator.

## 19. Complement 🎯 (keep it simple — this is all he wants)

**Complement** = a **series of proteins initiated as a cascade** (at least **26** blood proteins working
in concert). One protein gets "excited," excites the next, and the whole system fires **all at once** —
*"like a sparkler."* *(Slides 48–49; Transcript)*

**Functions — the testable four:**
1. **Neutralizes viruses**
2. **Enhances phagocytosis**
3. **Damages the plasma membrane** of the pathogen
4. **Lyses (destroys) the microorganism**

**Three pathways** — all reach a **similar end result** *(reference only for this course)*:
- **Classical**
- **Lectin**
- **Alternative**

> **He deliberately keeps it shallow:** *"complement is so complicated… for this course, just know it's a
> cascade of proteins that can neutralize viruses, destroy microorganisms, and enhance phagocytosis."*

---

## 20. Interferon 🎯

**Interferon (IFN)** = a protein **every cell** in the body can make in **tiny amounts**, with **antiviral
and anti-cancer** properties. It works by **interfering with the transcription and translation of mRNA**,
so viruses can't replicate. *(Slides 50–51)*

- **Three types:** interferon **alpha, beta, gamma** — all produced in response to viruses, RNA, immune
  products, and antigens; they bind cell surfaces and change gene expression, and can **suppress cancer
  genes** (tumor-suppressor effects). *(Slide 51)*
- **Clinical:** pooled interferon is sold as therapy — e.g., **Betaseron** (interferon beta) for **MS**
  flares. *(Transcript)*

---

## 21. Normal flora & natural resistance 🎯

**Non-specific biological barriers** *(Slides 39, 53)*:

- **Normal flora / microbiota** — the **commensal or mutualistic** organisms that live on/in you for life.
  They **out-compete** potential pathogens for **space and nutrients**, keeping pathogens in check.
- **Natural resistance** — resistance you **acquire throughout life** (as an adult), plus resistance a
  **child acquires from its mother.**

> **Why the immune system usually wins:** with intact barriers, flushing, chemicals, complement,
> interferon, **and** a protective normal flora, *"we almost never get sick."* *(Transcript)*

---

## 22. Self vs. non-self & white blood cells 🎯

Some white blood cells destroy foreign cells that enter the body. *(Slides 54–58)*

- **Immunology** = the study of the body's **2nd and 3rd lines of defense.** A healthy immune system
  performs **surveillance → recognition → destruction** of foreign material. *(Slide 55)*
- WBCs tell **self** from **non-self** by **examining markers on cell surfaces.** *(Slide 56)*
- **Non-self = antigen** — anything not part of the body. *(Transcript)*
- **Every cell is "marked" as you** — *"everything in James's body is marked James"* — **except red
  blood cells**, which use a different marking system (that's why blood can be **cross-typed and
  transfused**). *(Transcript)*

> **Transplant tie-in:** a donated kidney is marked with the **donor's** identity, so the recipient's
> immune system sees it as **non-self** and rejects it — hence **lifelong immunosuppressants**. *(Transcript)*

> 📷 **Slide 57** *(Figure 14.4)* — a WBC meeting a **self**-marked cell (leaves it alone) vs. a
> **non-self** cell (phagocytizes and destroys it).

| **Virulence factors (4)** | Structural · Toxins · Enzymes · Anti-phagocytic |
| **Exotoxin vs. endotoxin** | Secreted protein, heat-labile, toxoidable, no fever vs. LPS lipid A, heat-stable, fever, no vaccine |
