## 1. The digestive tract & accessory organs 🎯

The **digestive (gastrointestinal / GI) tract**, or **alimentary canal**, is one continuous tube from **mouth → anus**, ~**9 m (30 ft)** long. *(Slide 2)*

- **GI-tract organs (the tube):** oral cavity → pharynx → esophagus → stomach → small intestine → large intestine → anus.
- **Accessory organs** help digest but are **not part of the tube:** salivary glands, teeth, tongue, **liver, gallbladder, pancreas.** *(Slide 3)*

> **Plain-language:** the tract is the assembly line; the accessory organs are the tool-shops beside it that pass in enzymes, bile, and buffers.

---

## 2. Histology of the GI tract — the 4 layers 🎯

Every segment of the tube wall has the same **four layers** (inside → outside). *(Slide 4)*

1. **Mucosa** — the innermost lining; touches the food. **Always the histological focal point** — when the professor shows any GI image, look at the mucosa first.
2. **Submucosa** — connective tissue packed with **glands, vessels, nerves.**
3. **Muscularis externa** — smooth muscle; **where peristalsis/churning happens.**
4. **Serosa** — the outer wrapping.

> **Why the mucosa matters:** it's the layer in contact with the lumen contents, so its epithelium and cell types are what identify each organ on a slide.

---

## 3. Oral cavity — mechanical digestion begins 🎯

Together with the teeth, tongue, and salivary glands, the mouth: **mechanically digests** food, **lubricates** it with mucus/saliva, **begins (but does not finish) chemical digestion of carbohydrates**, and forms the **bolus** — a ball of food + saliva that is swallowed. *(Slide 5)*

- **Teeth** → **mastication** (mechanical breakdown).
- **Tongue** → rolls food into the **bolus** and mixes it with saliva.
- **Salivary amylase** starts breaking **carbohydrates** — *this is why a cracker held in the mouth turns sweet* (polysaccharides → monosaccharides like glucose/fructose you can taste).

> **Wait —** amylase is *always* aimed at **carbohydrates**. Keep that pairing locked in.

---

## 4. The three salivary glands 🎯 (identify macro + micro)

You must recognize all three both **macroscopically** (torso/model, lab practical) and **microscopically** (histology image). *(Slide 7)*

| Gland | Saliva it makes | Histology tell |
|---|---|---|
| **Parotid** (largest) | Watery, **enzyme-rich** (salivary amylase + **antimicrobial** enzymes) | **Serous cells** — dark-staining (enzymes take up stain well); "true glandular tissue" |
| **Sublingual** (under tongue) | **Mucus-rich** (coats the bolus for easy passage) | **Mucus cells** — pale/translucent, clustered in circles |
| **Submandibular** | **Mixed** — watery, enzyme-rich **and** mucous | Both serous and mucous cells |

- **Parotid job:** enzyme-rich saliva **moistens** the bolus, **protects** against microbes (antimicrobial enzymes), and **begins carbohydrate digestion.**
- **Sublingual job:** mucus **coats** the bolus so it slides through the throat and esophagus.

> **Wait —** on histology, "a cluster of **dark-staining** cells" = **parotid** (serous/enzyme). "Pale translucent circular clusters" = **sublingual** (mucus). The professor: *"Think of that as a question on the lecture exam."*

---

## 5. Esophagus — a purely peristaltic tube 🎯

The esophagus (~**10 in**) carries food from the pharynx to the stomach. It sits **posterior to the trachea** (a big swallowed bolus presses on the windpipe) and passes through the **esophageal hiatus** — the hole in the diaphragm. *(Slides 8–9)*

- **Peristalsis** = **adjacent layers of smooth muscle contract in a coordinated rhythm** to push material point A → point B. *(peri = around, stalsis = contractions)*
- The esophagus is a **"purely peristaltic organ"** — its only job is transport. **No real digestion happens here** (a little residual chemical activity at most).
- **Histology:** lined by **non-keratinized stratified squamous epithelium.**

> **Why that epithelium?** It's **tough** (many cell layers) so abrasive, low-pH food (a Dorito, a Mountain Dew) can't tear or burn through it — but **non-keratinized** because, unlike skin, it stays moist and isn't fighting water loss. Both **strength and protection.**

---

## 6. Sphincters & the lower esophageal (cardiac) sphincter 🎯

Movement through the GI tract is gated by **sphincters** — *"a big one on the exam."* First up: the **lower esophageal sphincter (LES)**, also called the **cardiac sphincter** (it sits near the heart). *(Slide 8)*

- **Job:** regulate movement between the esophagus and stomach.
- **While swallowing → relaxed** (food must pass). **Not swallowing → contracted** (keep acidic stomach contents down).
- **LES fails → reflux.** Acidic stomach contents creep up → burning = **heartburn / acid reflux**. On the exam call it **GERD (gastroesophageal reflux disease).**
- **Persistent GERD → Barrett's esophagus:** acid erodes the squamous epithelium, exposing/altering the underlying tissue. **Barrett's is the precursor to esophageal cancer** — treat aggressively (periodic endoscopy).
- **Risk factors for LES dysfunction:** obesity, hormonal surges (**pregnancy** — hCG/progesterone), stress, genetics.

> **Why GERD is worse at night:** you're lying flat (gravity drags acid up) **and** basal acid secretion peaks at night (see §11).

---

## 7. Stomach — churning, chyme, and (almost) no absorption 🎯

The **stomach** is a **J-shaped** organ under the diaphragm; it can stretch from **fist-sized to multiple liters.** *(Slide 10)*

- **Gross features:** **lesser** and **greater curvatures** (make the "J"); regions = **fundus** (dome), **cardiac**, **pyloric.**
- **Unique anatomy:** the muscularis externa has **three** smooth-muscle layers (most of the tube has two) → the stomach can **churn.**
- **Churning** = mechanical digestion; think of a **cement mixer**. It blends food with **hydrochloric acid (HCl)** and **digestive enzymes**, producing **chyme** (the acidic slurry — food + drink are no longer called "food and drink" once mixed).
- **Rugae (gastric folds)** let the stomach **stretch/expand** (not mainly to add surface area).
- **HCl denatures proteins** (unfolds the 3-D shape) so **pepsin** can reach the peptide bonds. Chemical digestion of protein **begins in the stomach.**

> **Wait — the stomach's job is NOT absorption.** Say "absorption" about the stomach on the exam and the professor deducts major points. It **stores** food briefly and **prepares macromolecules** for the small intestine. The few things it *does* absorb — **aspirin, alcohol** — are **gastric irritants** (common cause of ulcers).

---

## 8. Pyloric sphincter & gastric emptying 🎯

The **pyloric sphincter** controls chyme leaving the stomach for the **duodenum.** *(Slide 10)*

- **Gastric emptying** is slow and metered — **like one squirt of a squirt gun** at a time. Dumping all the acidic chyme at once would destroy the small intestine.
- **Pyloric stenosis** = a **narrowed** pyloric sphincter (common in newborns) → stomach can't empty → chyme/acid backs up; often surgically corrected.

---

## 9. Stomach histology — the gastric cells 🎯 (the "10-point" image)

The stomach mucosa contains **gastric pits/glands** lined by distinct cells. *This histology image is worth ~10 points on the lab practical plus multiple-choice.* Look at the **mucosa** only. *(Slides 11, 21)*

- **Mucus (neck) cells** — top of the pit; secrete **bicarbonate-rich, alkaline mucus** that coats and **protects** the lining from its own acid. *(You don't have to separate "surface" vs "neck" mucous cells — ⛔.)*
- **Parietal cells** — secrete **hydrochloric acid (HCl).**
- **Chief cells** — deepest; secrete the **inactive** enzyme **pepsinogen** (a "-gen" = inactive zymogen). HCl converts **pepsinogen → pepsin**, which digests proteins.
- **G cells** — enteroendocrine cells that release the hormone **gastrin.**

> **Why enzymes are released inactive:** an active protein-digesting enzyme would destroy the very cell that made it. Pepsinogen only becomes pepsin **once it meets HCl** in the lumen — the same rule holds for the pancreatic enzymes later (§17).

---

## 10. Gastric ulcers — always a mucus problem 🎯

A **gastric ulcer** is a hole where **HCl has eaten through the stomach lining.** It always traces back to **too little protective mucus.** *(Slide 21)*

1. **H. pylori** (*Helicobacter pylori*) — most common cause; its toxin **blocks mucus (neck) cells** from secreting mucus.
2. **Stress** — activates the **sympathetic** nervous system → **decreases** mucus production.
3. **Gastric irritants / NSAIDs** (e.g., **aspirin**) — reduce mucus. *(You don't need the prostaglandin mechanism — ⛔.)*

> **Plain-language:** mucus is the bubble-wrap over the stomach wall. Pop the bubble-wrap (bacteria, stress, or aspirin) and acid burns a hole.

---

## 11. Basal gastric acid secretion 🎯

**Basal (resting) gastric acid secretion follows a circadian rhythm:** **lowest in the morning, highest at night**, and continues during **interdigestive periods** (between meals — the longest is usually overnight). *(Slide 12)*

> **Why it matters clinically:** peak nighttime acid + lying flat = GERD flares are worst in bed.

---

## 12. Secretagogues of HCl & the proton pump 🎯

A **secretagogue** is a substance that triggers another cell to secrete something. **Three secretagogues** stimulate **parietal cells** to make HCl: *(Slides 14–16)*

| Secretagogue | Source / receptor | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **ACh (acetylcholine)** | **Vagus nerve (CN X), parasympathetic**; **M3** receptor | "Rest and digest" turns on acid |
| **Gastrin** | **G cells** of the stomach; **CCK(-B)** receptor | Stomach hormone → more acid |
| **Histamine** | Local cells; **H2** receptor | (Different from the H1 allergy receptor) |

- All three bind **G-protein-coupled receptors → cAMP →** the **H⁺/K⁺ ATPase ("proton pump")** relocates to the cell membrane and pumps **H⁺ (protons)** into the stomach. **Chloride follows** → **HCl.**
- A **proton** = a hydrogen atom that lost its electron (H⁺). So the pump moves protons.

**Pharmacology (know the drug targets):**
- **Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)** — **omeprazole, pantoprazole** — block the **H⁺/K⁺ pump** directly.
- **H₂ blockers** — **Tagamet (cimetidine)** — block the **histamine H2** receptor.
- Both lower stomach acid → used for **GERD** (less acid to reflux → protects the esophagus from Barrett's).

> **Note:** *"PEPCID"* (famotidine here described as acting like a base/Tums) neutralizes/buffers acid — a different strategy from blocking secretion.

---

## 13. Inhibition of gastric acid secretion 🎯

Acid gets turned **off** so the small intestine can keep up. *(Slide 17)*

- **Somatostatin** — released by gastric **D cells** — is the **primary** inhibitor of acid secretion.
- **Enterogastrones** = hormones from the small intestine: **secretin, CCK, GIP** (add **GLP-1**). **Every hormone made by the small intestine inhibits the stomach** — it slows parietal-cell HCl output **and** slows gastric emptying.

> **Why the small intestine brakes the stomach:** it needs time to work on chyme. If the stomach dumped everything at once, acidic chyme would overwhelm and damage the intestine.

---

## 14. Alkaline tide 🎯

For **every H⁺ a parietal cell pumps into the stomach, it moves a bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) into the blood.** So as the stomach lumen gets **more acidic**, the **blood leaving the stomach gets more alkaline** — the **alkaline tide** (blood leaving the stomach has a **higher/alkaline pH**). *(Slide 20)*

- **Excessive vomiting** → you lose stomach acid and keep base → **metabolic alkalosis.**
- **Diarrhea** → you lose base (bicarbonate) and keep acid → **metabolic acidosis.**

> **Memory hook:** **vomit = alkalosis, diarrhea = acidosis** — they push the body to **opposite** metabolic states.

## 15. The three digestive phases 🎯 (15–18-point short answer)

A meal triggers **three phases** of acid/secretion. Know what turns each one on and what it does. *(Slides 13, 18–19, 22–23)*

**① Cephalic phase — anticipatory ("in the head").**
- Triggered by the **sight, smell, or thought of food — before anything enters the GI tract.**
- The **parasympathetic** system (**vagus nerve, CN X**) tells the stomach to prep: **mucus cells → mucus**, **chief cells → pepsinogen**, **parietal cells → HCl.**

**② Gastric phase — food is in the stomach.**
- **Stretch** of the stomach wall (distension) reinforces the **PSNS/vagus** → even more mucus, pepsinogen, HCl.
- **Pepsin** breaks proteins into small peptides; **G cells** detect those peptides → release **gastrin** → parietal cells make **even more HCl.**
- Churning mixes everything into **chyme.**

**③ Intestinal phase — chyme enters the duodenum.**
- The **small intestine now coordinates all the other digestive organs.** Enteroendocrine cells release hormones (§16) that **slow the stomach** and **direct the pancreas and gallbladder.**

> **Wait —** the intestinal phase is weighted the heaviest. The professor: *"REALLY KNOW WHAT EACH OF THESE HORMONES DOES AT THEIR TARGET CELLS."* Cephalic/gastric ≈ 7 pts each; intestinal ≈ 10.

## 16. Intestinal-phase hormones — targets & jobs 🎯

Every small-intestine hormone **targets the stomach and slows it down** — plus its own extra targets. *(Slides 17, 22–23, 26)*

| Hormone | Released in response to | Targets & effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Secretin** | **Acid** (low pH), by **S cells** | **Stomach** (slow down) + **pancreas** (release **bicarbonate**-rich juice that buffers chyme and protects the intestinal lining) |
| **CCK** (cholecystokinin) | **Fats & proteins** | **Broadest — 3 targets:** **stomach** (slow), **pancreas** (release **digestive enzymes**), **gallbladder** (**contract → bile → emulsify fat**) |
| **GIP** (gastric inhibitory peptide) | **Carbohydrates / glucose** | **Stomach** + **pancreas** |
| **GLP-1** (glucagon-like peptide-1) | **Carbohydrates** | **Slows gastric emptying** (you feel full → eat less) and tells pancreatic **β-cells → insulin** |

> **Note (clinical tie-in):** **Ozempic / Wegovy** are **GLP-1** drugs — they slow gastric emptying (prolonged fullness → weight loss) and raise insulin. Common side effects follow directly: **nausea/vomiting** and **constipation** (the whole GI tract slows).

## 17. The pancreas — endocrine vs. exocrine 🎯

The pancreas has **two jobs.** *(Slides 24–28)*

- **Endocrine pancreas** = **islets of Langerhans** — secrete **hormones (insulin, glucagon) into the blood.**
- **Exocrine pancreas:**
  - **Acinar cells** → **digestive enzymes** (triggered by **CCK**).
  - **Duct cells** → **sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃)** → the alkaline **pancreatic juice** that buffers acidic chyme (triggered by **secretin**).

**Zymogen activation (a safety cascade):** the pancreas secretes enzymes **inactive.** In the small intestine, **brush-border enteropeptidase** converts **trypsinogen → trypsin**, and **trypsin** then activates the others (chymotrypsinogen → chymotrypsin, procarboxypeptidase → carboxypeptidase, etc.). *(Slide 28)*

**How bile & pancreatic juice reach the gut (lab-practical ID):** the **common bile duct** (bile from liver + gallbladder via the **cystic** and **common hepatic ducts**) and the **main pancreatic duct** join at the **hepatopancreatic ampulla (of Vater)**, gated by the **hepatopancreatic sphincter (of Oddi)**, and empty into the duodenum at the **major duodenal papilla.** *(Slide 24)*

> **Why inactive:** same logic as pepsinogen — active protein-digesting enzymes would digest the pancreas itself. They only switch on **in the lumen.**

## 18. The liver 🎯

The liver's headline digestive job is **bile.** *(Slides 29–36)*

- **Bile synthesis** — bile **emulsifies fat** (breaks large fat droplets into small ones = chemical digestion of fat).
- **Hepatic portal circulation** — **filters blood leaving the GI tract** for pathogens and toxins (via **Kupffer cells**, resident macrophages).
- **Stores glucose as glycogen** (and releases it when needed).
- **Broader roles:** metabolism (glycogenesis/glycogenolysis/gluconeogenesis; lipid & amino-acid processing → **ammonia → urea**), **detox** (drugs via cytochrome P450; alcohol via alcohol dehydrogenase), **synthesis** (albumin, clotting factors), **bilirubin** processing, and **storage** of iron and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) + B12.

**Anatomy to identify:**
- **Macroscopic** *(Slide 30)*: **lobes** (right, left, caudate, quadrate), **falciform ligament**, **ligamentum teres**, **porta hepatis**, hepatic **portal vein / artery / ducts**, gallbladder.
- **Microscopic** *(Slides 31–34)*: the **lobule** with a **central vein**; at each corner a **portal triad** (portal vein, hepatic artery, bile duct); **plates of hepatocytes**; **sinusoids** (fenestrated) with **Kupffer cells**; **bile canaliculi** carrying bile toward the bile ducts.

## 19. The gallbladder 🎯

The **gallbladder stores and concentrates bile** and releases it when signaled **hormonally by CCK** (when fat is in the gut). *(Slides 38–39)*

> **Why it forms stones:** bile sits and is **concentrated** here, so its components (cholesterol, bile salts, bilirubin) can precipitate into **gallstones.**

---

## 20. The small intestine — structure & absorption 🎯

The small intestine is where **most chemical digestion finishes and most absorption happens.** *(Slides 40–46)*

**Regions (proximal → distal):** **duodenum** (first ~25 cm) → **jejunum** (~2.5 m) → **ileum** (~3.5 m); ~6 m total.

**Surface-area features (each amplifies the next):** *(Slides 42–43)*
- **Plicae circulares (circular folds)** — big folds of the mucosa/submucosa.
- **Villi** — finger-like projections; each holds a **capillary** (to blood) and a **lacteal** (a lymphatic vessel, for fats).
- **Microvilli (brush border)** — on each absorptive cell; hold brush-border enzymes (e.g., **enteropeptidase**).
- **Intestinal crypts, goblet cells, enteroendocrine cells** sit in the mucosa too.

**Absorption of the three macronutrients:**
- **Carbohydrates** *(Slide 44)* — broken to **monosaccharides** by amylase then **maltase/sucrase/lactase.** **Glucose & galactose** enter on **SGLT** (with **Na⁺**), **fructose** enters on **GLUT5**; all exit on **GLUT2** to the capillary.
- **Proteins** *(Slide 45)* — **endopeptidases** (pepsin, trypsin, chymotrypsin) cut internal bonds; **exopeptidases** (amino-/carboxypeptidase) trim the ends. Amino acids **cotransport with Na⁺**; di-/tripeptides with **H⁺**; some larger peptides cross by **transcytosis.** Absorbed to blood → **liver.**
- **Fats** *(Slide 46)* — **bile salts** coat fat droplets (**emulsion**) → **pancreatic lipase + colipase** make **monoglycerides + fatty acids** packaged in **micelles** → diffuse into the cell → repackaged with cholesterol/protein into **chylomicrons** → exit into the **lacteal** (lymph), not the capillary.

> **Note — SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth):** *(Slide 41)* colon bacteria migrate into the ileum → bloating/gas, **malabsorption** (fat-soluble vitamins, B12), diarrhea, "leaky gut," inflammation.

---

## 21. The large intestine — water, microbes, gross anatomy 🎯

The colon's main job is **absorbing water and electrolytes** ("big daddy" of water reclamation); **very little digestion** happens here, and what does is **done by bacteria.** *(Slides 47–48)*

- **Gross anatomy:** **cecum → appendix → colon → rectum → anal canal.**
- **Gut microbiome** — the resident bacteria aid digestion of fiber/complex carbs, support the **immune** barrier, and interact with metabolism and the **gut-brain axis**. *(Deep probiotic-benefit lists on Slide 50 are reference-only.)*

---

## 22. The defecation reflex 🎯

Feces entering the **rectum** stretches the wall → the reflex fires. *(Slide 52)*

1. **Stretch receptors** in the rectal wall signal the spinal cord.
2. A **spinal (parasympathetic) reflex** contracts the rectal wall and **relaxes the internal anal sphincter** (**smooth muscle — involuntary**).
3. The **external anal sphincter** (**skeletal muscle — voluntary**, under cerebral-cortex control) decides whether it's convenient; if so, it relaxes and feces pass.

> **Memory hook:** **Internal = Involuntary** (smooth), **External = under your control** (skeletal, voluntary).
